  • Safety fence with caution marks surrounding the work area is required.

  • Wear the personal protective equipment must used as safety shoes, safety helmet, earplug, and gloves pairs ... etc

  • Shut down the unit and be out of service

  • Isolate compressed air for all valves

  • Switch off gland exhaust and oil vapor extractor

  • Switch off ventilation system

  • Generator should be isolated

Work to be Carried Out

Preparation Required for Steam Valves (Disassembly)

  1. Remove insulation cover.

  2. Remove valve cover.

  3. Remove steam strainer.

  4. Disassemble valve position lever and coupling.

  5. Disassemble hydraulic operation steam.

  6. Remove valve steam bushing assembly.

  7. Disassemble valve stem bushing assembly.

    8.

Remove Insulation Cover

  1. Remove sheet metal fasteners at the horizontal joint.

  2. Install eye bolts.

  3. Rig and lift the cover straight up.

Remove Valve Cover

  1. All cop studs are of the bottoming variety, cop studs user on higher temperature pressure studs are equipped with bolt heater holes.

  2. Remove all cover nuts with on impact wrench or slugging wrench and sledge hammer use bolt heaters where possible to aid in nut removal  in very  difficult cases, you may need to cut the nut off using an air arc.

    3.

Remove Steam Strainer

  1. Install eye bolts in these holes and the steam strainer with chain fall and cable attached to these eyebolts.

  2. Make a vertical lift, using the chain fall until the basket is clear of the valve.

Disassemble Valve Position Lever and Coupling

  1. There are two configurations of valve position levers and couplings used on stop valves – to ensure positive valve closing the hydraulic piston shaft is usually pre–added you must release this loading by loosening the coupling bolts in the coupling. The bolts are usually long enough to relive this pressure.

  2. Disassemble the valve position lever and coupling. The mechanical hydraulic control ( MHC ) figure 17 design configuration comes apart easily after the bolts removed. The electro hydraulic control ( EHC ) figure 18 design requires jacking up the spring retainer under the spring to remove the split washer. With the washer removed, the spring tension will be released up on removal of the jacking device.

Disassemble Hydraulic Operation Steam

  1. Disconnect both the oil feed and drain lines.

  2. Loosen all operating cylinder horizontal joint bolts, two or three of these bolts should be longer than the others.

  3. Remove these long bolts last, as they are designed to relive the piston spring tension.

  4. Once of the operating cylinder is lowered off the piston, rig and remove the housing.

Remove Valve Steam Bushing Assembly

  1. Unlock the jam nuts.

  2. Loosen all pull down ring bolts

  3. Rotate off pull down ring.

  4. Attach rigging, using the three eyebolt hole in the top of the valve disk.

  5. Use a chain fall to make the vertical lift. Don't use the crane until the guide bushing is out of its valve body seat.

  6. The valve stem will rise freely until the stem makes contact with its back seat. Continue applying vertical pressure. If this pressure does not break the bushing assembly from the housing, it will be necessary to use a jack. Place the jack between the hydraulic actuator and the valve stem.

Disassemble Valve Steam Bushing Assembly

  1. Remove the main valve disc cover bolts. These are staked.

  2. Lubricate the heads with oil.

  3. Twist the bolts past the penning. The cover will come of easily once the bolts are removed. The pilot valve and bypass valve will almost fall out without the cover to hold them in place.

  4. Push the valve stem up into the main valve disk until the split washer holding the stem to the disk can be removed.

  5. Push the stem in the opposite direction. This will remove the stem from the valve disk and expose an anti – rotation pin in the stem.

  6. Remove the anti – rotation pin and continue sliding the valve stem out of the bushing assembly.

Valve Disks

  1. Valve disks must be destructively tested

  2. Dye penetrant check the following parts for cracking, the satellite inlay anti – rotation lugs and the inner radius on bar lift control valves.

  3. Visually inspect the valve disk for foreign particle erosion, foreign object damage, pitting, missing satellite inlay and or missing valve bolts and anti – rotation pins.

 

 

Valve Seats

  1. Valve disks must also be non – destructively tested during inspection.

  2. Dye penetrant check the satellite and seal weld areas for cracking. The purpose of seal weld, on all stop and intercept valves is two fold. First the weld  is a back – up seal. Second, it is a method of locking to keep the seat in place it prevents the seat from spinning or lifting should the shrink fit be lost, either temporally, during transient operating conditions, or permanently, due to over stressing of the parts during severe temperature changes.

  3. Visually inspect the valve seat for foreign particle erosion foreign object damage and missing bolts (for bolted in seats only).

  4. Contact checks between all valve disks to seats must be taken at every inspection, the areas required contact checks includes:
    a- Main seat to disk.       b- By-pass or equalizer seat to disk         c- Stem seal ( back seat ).

  5. The acceptable amount of contact is a continuous line a continuous line around the valve seat 1/16" to 1/8" wide. Two steps are required to perform these contact checks:

  1. Blue the valve and place it on the seat to determine contact between the valve and the seat. The valve disk should not be rotated but tapped only lightly to transfer the bluing from the valve disk to the seat.

  2. Examine the contact line on the valve seat. If the contact line doesn't show good contact, re–blue the valve and place it on the seat with the temporary match marks 180° apart. Tap the valve for contact and examine the contact line. If the contact marks didn't move, the valve seat must be reseated. If the contact marks rotated the valve must be reseated.

Valve Stems         Sheet no.2  MRDS

  1. Stems must be non – destructively tested during valve component inspection.

  2. Check the valves steam for cracks, using the red – dye method whenever valves are disassembled for maintenance. The areas most likely to have cracks are locations such as anti – rotation pin holes keyways, the roots of threads, and the inside area of corner fillets, particularly in the stem seal area. Generally, cracks along the stem axis will not be serious enough to cause scrapping of the stem scrapping the stem is only necessary when stem cracks are large and open .

  3. Stop valve steam erosion has been reported on many main stop valves designed for full – arc admission operation. The erosion  takes place in a region just below the by pass – valve disk and is caused by solid oxide particles in the steam. The amount of erosion varies greatly from one turbine to another and is a function of the steam coming from the boiler.

    Valve stems should also be visually inspected to :

    * Galling.                                              * Excessive oxide chipping or build up.

    * Foreign object damage.                     * Pounding out of stem seal.

    * Chipped worn or deformed threads.

    4.

  4. A stem run out check must be taken on all valve stems, use a pair of " V " blocks lubricated with at least 30 w oil, with one block on the stem diameter near the disk and the other block on the stem diameter near the wrench flats, find the maximum total indicator run out that exists at some point between the blocks. Total indicator run out is defined as the total indicator reading ( TIR ) at the point of maximum bending when the ends of the stem are running true ( zero run out). Steam Strainers

Visual inspection is usually sufficient on steam strainers the most common defects encountered include:

 

* Broken screen wires.

* Cracked welds.

* Broken or missing rivets.

* Foreign objects in screen.

 

When you find dimpling or foreign objects in the screen, clean the affected area.

When you find broken screen wires, cracked welds, or broken or missing rivets, contact Lynn for repair recommendations.

Linkages

  1. Inspect valve linkages for the following condition:

    * Interference

    * Freedom of movement

    * Bent or distorted links.

    * Loose ball joint

    * Loose fitting pins
    * Fretted or galled pins and pushing's * Missing pin retainers ( i.e. anti–rotation bars )

Valve Studs        Figures no. 3, 11, 12, 13, 14, and 15

  1. The valve studs should be inspected at least every 6 years, and more often if they are retightened frequented if the studs can be easily removed after being soaked in penetrating oil, they should be magnetic particle inspected if the studs cannot be easily removed, the ultrasonic inspection method described below is recommended to avoid the risk and effort required to remove stuck studs. Ultrasonic inspection is a superior method of stud inspection, if indication are evident with ultrasonic testing, remove the stud.

  2. Don’t attempt to remove studs unless it is absolutely necessary. Many outages have been unnecessarily extended because a good stud was galled half way out of its hole and had to be cut before it could be completely removed.

Ultrasonic Testing of Steam Valve Studs after Period of Service  Sheet no. 6 MRDS

  1. Tests should be performed by well – trained and properly qualified personnel.

  2. Perform tests while the studs are in place and generally, after the cover has been removed. However where the end of the stud protrudes above the nut , the studs may be tested without disassembly.

  3. Use Sperry reflect scope, type UR or equivalent. The ultrasonic instrument vertical presentation shall be linear within ± 5 percent of the full scale deflection.

  4. The exposed end of each stud should be free of all scale and oxide. Caution should be exercised to maintain a flat surface for search unit contact.

  5. Use suitable compliant, such as SAE 20 oil.

  6. Adjust the distance calibration markers on a calibration bar of material similar to that of the studs, for accurate distance measurements.

    7.

Method of Testing

  1. Perform tests using a 5.0 MC, 1/2 or 1 inch diameter type ZR search unit experience has shown that these search units give the best results. However, due to variations in type of material and geometry, it may be necessary to use search units of other frequencies and diameters.

  2. Adjust the sweep length until the first back reflection of the stud is visible on the right hand side of the oscilloscope screen.

    3.

  3. Measure and record the distance from the top of the stud to they alye joint surface. Mark the oscilloscope screen at this measure distance the results obtained to date demonstrate that crack indications will appear at the first or second thread, 0. 1/4 inch below the valve joint surface as shown in figure 12 and 13.

  4. Adjust the sensitivity until the amplitude of the indications from the valve end threads is 5 percent of 1/2 inches, sweep to peak.

  5. Test the studs completely from the exposed end by scanning 360 ° in a see – saw manner as shown in figure 14

  6. Assign each stud a number indexed with respect to the down pin as shown in figure 15.

  7. Repeat the scanning procedure on all studs.

  8. Mark all indications on the test surfaces as they occur. Indicate the circumferential distribution of indications using a clock system the 12 O'clock position on each stud is toward the open direction of the valve and in radial line with the center of the valve bore.

Cracked Studs

  1. If any cracked studs in a given valve are more than six years old, they should be replaced before restarting.

  2. All other studs in that valve and in mating valves should be replaced at the earliest opportunity when cracks are found in studs less than six years old product service should be consulted for recommendation. Since the cracks may result.

Valve Casings

  1. The following non-destructive tests should be performed on valve casings during an inspection.

  2. It is generally recommended that stop and control valve casing be given a through magnetic particle inspection after approximately 6 years of service, and that subsequent inspection be conducted at 4 years intervals

  3. Steam dams ( anti – swirl partitions ) in valve wound gasket the final pull down height for the gasket should be 0.125 ± 0.005  inch ( initial gasket thickness is 0.175 inch + 0.005 inch ) the gasket sealing surface should be undamaged.

Stop Valve Cap Bolt

  1. Cap bolts may be damaged during maintenance damaged cap bolts should not be reused. The chunk and threads  should be red – dye checked after cleaning. Cracks are not acceptable visually inspect the following areas of the stop valve cap bolt:

    1. Check threads for damages

    2. Check Hex socket for crushed sockets.

    3. Check Head bearing surface for galled head bearing surface.

Cam Shafts

  1. The Cam shaft should be inspected for run out and journal surface condition. Take micrometer readings, note journal size variations, and compare these measurements to the original shaft size and to the measurements recorded on the last service report. The following three faders will determine if shaft replacement is necessary:

    1. With Teflon bearings, the shaft should hold taper and shaft roundness to  0.0015 inch on all journals. Depending on the wear patter non the bearing journal irregularities may be increased to 0.003 inch.

    2. With grease lubricated brass or bronze bearings, shaft irregularities tend to stabilize with tapers and out of round conditions in journal areas near 0.008 inch units have been found with taper and out of round conditions in journal areas as large as 0.0221 inch.

    3. Graphite  impregnated bearings hold tolerances some where between the Teflon and greased bearing types. Due to the unusual torque loading on cam shafts during operation, you should recommend changing shafts whenever the journal surfaces will cause new bearings to wear so that they will fit the poor shaft conditions.

Cam Shaft Bearings

  1. Clean and micrometer readings on the condition of each bearing as well as the cam shaft, This measurement and the wear pattern will dictate if the bearing should be replaced or realigned.

  2. Replace Teflon bearings every 4 to 6 years. Graphite impregnated and grease type bearings should be replaced when they wear out and before they begin to cut the cam shaft.

Cam Rollers

  1. Rollers should be inspected to ensure that thy have a smooth, round surface, and to ensure that bushing to pin clearances are within specified to learners, if either of these factors change, the valve timing will changes well, the roller pin to bushing clearance should be between 0.002 to 0.006 inch clear.

  2. No galling or pin wear should be noted. if the roller OD is worn or has flat spots, have it trued for roundness, don't true a roller if it will reduce the OD by more than 0.125 inch, recommend replacement in this case.

Lifting Lever

  1. Excessive clearance at the hole or bushing that holds the cam roller pins will change valve timing sequences and increase working damage to all parts in that area.

  2. The hole and pins should be round with a 0.0005 to 0.003 inch clear once, depending on the type of pin used.

  3. The pivot at the hinge rod should also be checked for wear the clearances should be 0.003 to 0.005 inch.

Operating Cylinders and Power Activators

  1. All operating cylinder collect dirt readily. If the oil system is reasonably clean. However, the piston rings and cylinder housing will not experience undue wear.

  2. To inspect these components, take micrometer readings on the cylinder.

  3. Take this reading in the piston operating area in the operating cylinder and take butt clearance measurements on the piston rings.

  4. Clean the dirt trapped on the top of the operating piston. This cavity helps to keep the oil system clean.

  5. When removing the piston rings for butt clearances be careful not to break ring.

Pilot Valves and Bushings

  1. The pilot valve and bushing should be measured with a micrometer and visually checked for damage and scoring in the land areas.

  2. Check the lock, not holding the pilot valve in a fixed position to the control leakage is secure, any movement at this location is manifested as a control hunt or control response over shooting.

Rack and Pinion

  1. The rack and pinion should be checked for tooth wear and timing marks, tooth contact should be full and back-lash should be 0.010 to 0.0151 inch.

  2. If no control problems are noted, the back-lash may be allowed to increase, due to normal wear.

  3. Ensure that timing marks have been properly sequenced, especially  if either or both components have been replaced.

Spiral – Wound Gaskets

  1. All main steam lines, and most main stop valve cover flanges have spiral – wound gaskets with both inner and outer spacers. the inner spacer is about 0.100 inch thick. The gasket is therefore compressed to 0.125 inch when the joint is made up. the gasket is under a stress of 15.000 psi since it has been compressed from its original thickness of 0.175 – inch to 0.125 inch.

  2. When assembling steam valves, clean the components and install new spiral-wound and iron gaskets, where applicable. Tap the gasket to the male fit with transparent cellophane tape to prevent damage to gaskets during component assembly spiral-wound gasket are installed without any joint compound, they are known therefore, as dry joints.

Came Shaft and Bearings

  1. When the cam shaft and bearing are assembled, all journals and bearings should make contact.

  2. If this condition does not exist, you will have to use shim under the total bearing or bushing strong back. level the shaft, using the outer most bearing packs. then bring the other bearing packs up to the shaft for final contact

Rack and Pinion

  1. It is essential that timing marks on the rock and pinion match up, when new parts are installed, make sure that they have the same timing sequence as the old parts .

Soft Valve Stem Packing

  1. Always replace soft valve stem packing with the proper packing and the proper number of rings. Snug down the packing gland to ensure that the packing has just seated.

  2. Do not compress the packing to the point where the valve does not move.

  3. Pulling down a packing to stop a stem leak is preferable to finding a leak after the gland collar has been loosened to restore valve movement. Install these packing dry do not use a joint lubricant on the packing or stem.

Preparation of the unit for Start after Inspection

  1. Check the availability of DC& AC power supplies.

  2. Check the level of main tank lube oil in normal level.

  3. Operate the emergency lube oil pump check direction of rotation and the discharge pressure .

  4. Check lube oil flow through sight glass at bearing no. ( 1,2,3,4 )

  5. Check the level of cooling water tanks.

  6. Check valves position.

 
 

Check the Unit for

  1. Leakage of oil line

  2. Leakage of cooling and seal steam pipes.

  3. Abnormal noise or excessive vibration.

  4. Operate the unit at different loads.

  5. Take complete operation reading and record the results in the attached record sheet
