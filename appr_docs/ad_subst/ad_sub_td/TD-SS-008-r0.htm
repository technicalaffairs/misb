Introduction

Incipient faults in oil-immersed transformers are usually the result of electrical or thermal stress in either the insulating oil or the insulating materials. The development of a fault is known to create a mixture of gases characteristic of that fault. For example, the presence of C2H4 indicates a problem that causes the insulating oil to overheat.

Hitachi recommend that gaseous mixtures in insulating oil be analyzed by gas chromatograph or other means, both when the transformer is initially operated and at regular 12-months interval thereafter.

Information

Sampling transformer insulating oil

When sampling oil from a transformer, be especially careful to ensure that the oil flowing into the sampling container is clean and free of contamination.

The container should also be clean, and no air, foreign matter, or dirty oil should be allowed to contaminate the sample. To make sure that a sample is as representative as possible of the oil in the tank, first remove 0.5 to 1.0 liter and dispose of it, then take the actual sample.

An oil sampling container such as the one illustrated in MPIS Document Nos. T-001-r0 & TD-SS-003-r0 can be used to maintain sample quality or as the one illustrated in Figure 1 below. Remember, oil sample should not be exposed to air before analysis.

Assessing test results

Including carbon monoxide, the total volume of combustible gases (TCG) that in Japan is considered indicated of a transformer fault 800 ppm to 2,000 ppm of oil, depending on the transformer rating. Further, even a small amount of C2H2 (acetylene) is also considered to indicate a faulty condition. Besides absolute values, any rapid increase of gas concentration since the previous test should also be considered suspicious.
