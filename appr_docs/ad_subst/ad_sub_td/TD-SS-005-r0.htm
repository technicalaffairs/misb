Introduction

SF6 gas-filled switching equipment is now in widespread use throughout EEA's transmission and distribution system.

This type of equipment can be expected to experience a gradual loss of SF6 gas due to normal leakage and occasionally, a larger, more rapid gas loss as a result of equipment failure

For these situations a reliable supply of replacement SF6 gas is required.

Directives

Physical Properties of Sulpher Hexa Fluoride

  • Pure SF6 is an inert, colorless, odorless, non-toxic gas, approx. five times heavier than air.

  • It is highly stable both chemically and thermally and is non-flammable.

  • At normal atmospheric pressure and ambient temperature SF6 is gaseous but will liquefy at relatively high temperature when subjected to high pressure.

  • When pressurized above 18 bars the gas will liquefy at normal ambient temperature.

Electrical Characteristics

  • SF6 has excellent dielectric properties.

  • The dielectric strength of SF6 gas at 2 bars is more than twice that of air at the same pressure and exceeds that of insulating oil.

  • The dielectric strength increases as gas pressure is increased

Storage of SF6 Cylinders

  • SF6 is normally supplied in 50 kg quantity inside standard steel cylinders. The cylinders must be stored in accordance with EEA's rules and regulations for pressurized gas containers.

  • Cylinders must be stored in an upright position and secured by chains to prevent them from falling over.

  • The control valves of the storage cylinders must always be covered by a protective cap that is securely screwed into its proper position.

  • Cylinders must be stored in a well-ventilated area, protected from direct sunlight and moisture

  • Large bulk storage cylinders are usually stored on their sides in specially designed storage racks inside GIS buildings.

 

Gas Handling

  • SF6 is usually received from the manufacturer in liquid form in standard gas cylinders. The pressure will vary from 7 bars to 35 bars depending on the temperature.

  • Since the pressure within the cylinder is dependent on the temperature, the quantity of SF6 in the cylinder cannot be measured by the pressure gauge.

  • The best indication of remaining contents inside the cylinder can be obtained by weighing the cylinder and subtracting the empty cylinder tare weight.

  • The safe removal of gaseous SF6 from the cylinders requires the use of a reliable, single stage pressure regulator with a delivery pressure range of 0 bar to 10 bars.

  • In order to remove the SF6 in gaseous form, it is necessary to evaporate the liquid inside the cylinder.

  • This process must be done very slowly to avoid a rapid temperature drop due to the evaporation of a gas from liquid state to gaseous state.

Gas Removal from Gas Insulated Switchgear Compartments

  • During work on internal components of gas-insulated switch-ear, it is necessary to remove the SF6 from the compartment to be maintained.

  • Since it is wasteful to vent the gas to atmosphere it is usual practice to pump it out of the compartment and store it in an S176 gas service cart for reuse.

  • This gas service cart has facilities for drying and filtering the gas when it is returned to the switchgear.

  • There are no known restrictions on the disposal of uncontaminated SF6 by discharge into the atmosphere, but for economic reasons this practice should be avoided.

PROXICITY

  • SF6, in both pure and commercial grades, is physiologically inert. It has no adverse effects when inhaled at high concentrations (in air) or for prolonged periods of time.

  • SF6 contains no oxygen and therefore will not support life, making it unsafe to work in any unventilated building containing SF6. Furthermore SF6 is heavier than air and will displace air in confined areas.

  • When exposed to sustained or intense electric arcs, SF6 decomposes to form Sulphur-Flourine gases and metallic fluorides. If moisture is also present, the decomposition product may include Sulphur - Oxy Fluorides and hydrofluoric acid.

  • These byproducts, especially hydrofluoric acid, can cause irritation to the respiratory system and to the eyes.

  • Fortunately, these compounds possess a very strong smell similar to rotten eggs and can therefor readily be detected. These gaseous byproducts can be dispersed easily using suitable forced ventilation.

Removal and Disposal of Solid Decomposition Products

  • The production of gaseous contaminants in sF6 may - in some cases - be accompanied by the formation of metallic fluorides; however this can only happen in compartments in which along term partial discharge or an internal severe power fault has occurred or possibly, inside a circuit breaker which has been subjected to repeated short circuit fault clearing operations.
     

  • Any Powdery deposits found upon opening a SF6 gas compartment must be treated as metallic fluorides. This powder must be removed promptly, using efficient vacuum cleaners, since the Powder will quickly absorb moisture and become corrosive.

  • During the removal of these powders the technician shall wear disposable coveralls and gloves and shall be protected by a full-face mask respirator with filler.

Additional Safety Precautions

  • SF6 gas used in switchgear and other equipment is always under pressure Circuit breakers and. gas filled compartments should be considered as pressure vessels. Inspection and other access covers must not be removed until the internal pressure has been reduced to atmospheric pressure.

  • SF6 gas is heavier than air and it will tend to collect at low point of buildings, in basements and cable vaults, displacing the air in that area. This can become very dangerous to personnel entering these areas since it could lead to oxygen starvation and possible suffocation. It is advisable to use a SF6 detector to detect the presence of SF6 gas in such areas. Forced ventilation may be used to purge the areas prior to entering.

  • In the unlikely event of an internal fault, which ruptures or burns through the enclosure, extra precautions should be taken to avoid exposure to the arc decomposition products. The faulted equipment must be approached with caution and if the strong smell of hydrofluoric acid is present, the immediate area should be evacuated and thoroughly vented until the odor subsides.
