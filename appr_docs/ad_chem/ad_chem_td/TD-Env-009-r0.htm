Introduction

The model R plant is a complete system for sewage and trade effluent treatment utilizing the activated sludge process.

Sewage flows into the aeration unit and is mixed with suspension of living micro–organisms (activated sludge) and aerated to maintain efficient mixing and provide the oxygen which the organisms need. The micro-organisms use the polluting matter in the sewage as food and convert it to carbon dioxide, water and new cell material.

After several hours aeration (from 8–12h), the mixture passes to settlement tank where the activated sludge settles and is returned to the aeration tank for reuse. The clear liquid on the surface of the settlement tank can be discharged as treated effluent without nuisance.

Because of the build up of micro–organism, the weight of activated sludge in the plant gradually increases. Periodically, therefore, it is necessary to remove or ‘waste’ some of this sludge to maintain the correct balance out of this document we discursive the settling and tertiary treatment.

Settling Operation

The fresh sewage entering the plant displaces mixed liquor from the contact zone into the settling inlet stilling well via pipe. The sludge falls to the button of settling basin and is scraped to the central sludge well by the mechanically driven scraper. Supernatant liquid from the central basin flows over the notched weir to the effluent through which it leaves the plant through the outlet pipe.

Grease and anther floating matter in the central settling basin is directed by mechanically driven skimmer into a scum removal mechanism from which it is automatically transferred to the aerobic digester. Sludge is drawn from the settling basin central sludge well by the air lift to enter the re-aeration zone.

Settlement Compartment

The central settlement tank is of the Upward Flow type and is fitted with an inlet stilling well, a mechanically driven sludge scraper at the bottom of mechanical surface skimmer and scum ejector.

The scraper is driven through a gear box and chain drive and incorporates a shear pin to protect the mechanism from accidental overload.

Description of Tertiary Treatment Plant

Biologically treated effluent from the treatment plants gravitates into a balancing sump. The effluent is filtered and chlorinated before discharge. The effluent quality from the treatment plants is on average 20/30 standard and in consequence contains 30 mg/l of fine suspended  solids, 20 mg /l of BOD and vast amounts of micro–organisms.

The tertiary treatment plant has been supplied to improve the effluent quality. This is accomplished by removing almost all of the suspended solids and BOD by filtration and sterilizing by chlorination.

Filtration System

Installed within the plant room are two (duty/standby) dry well filter feed pumps which transfer the effluent through pipe work and values into the top of the dual media filter.

The filter comprises a pressure vessel complete with a series of pneumatically operated values and pipe manifold.

Internally the filter has header and lateral pipe work system and is filled with various layers of gravel, sand and anthracite, the effluent passes through this media and the suspended solids become entrained. The filtered effluent is collected by the internal bleeder and lateral pipe system from where it is discharged into the chlorine contact tank.

As the filtered effluent enters the contact tank it is dosed with a dilute solution of sodium hypochlorite which kills off the micro-organism.

Periodically it is necessary to remove the accumulation of suspended solids from the filter.

This backwashing is achieved automatically by first of all passing relatively large amounts of low pressure compressed air up through the filter to loosen the entrained solids and expand the media. Effluent water is then passed up through the media removing the solids which are returned the filter is backwashed at least once per day.

Chlorination

The calcium hypochlorite is mixed to a 2% solution in a 200 litre mixing/ storage tank provided. This dilute solution is then pumped by a small dosing pump into the filtered effluent as it leaves the filter. In the auto position the dosing pump starts/stops in conjunction with the duty Filter Feed Pumps. From time to time it is necessary to control slime build up in the filter; this is accomplished by temporarily repiping the chlorination discharge to the balancing tank.

An air blower has been provided to supply low pressure compressed air for the air scour of the Filter prior to backwashing.

Directives

Maintenance of Settlement Tank.

The effluent weir in the settlement tank should be kept clean and free from algae by brushing, the roller on the end of the scum removal arm should be greased every month using grease.

The sludge return air lift should be in operation all the time,  and if the airlift become blocked it must be freed immediately.

Chemical Test

Suspended Matter: ( This Test in Document No. Water- 033-r0)

The amount of suspended matter should not increase by more than 0.25 mg/liter, 0.75 mg/liter.

The amount of suspended matter in water of open lodes (containing over 30 mg/liter of natural mineral substances) may increase, in normal level periods, by 5 per cent.

Suspensions, settling at rates exceeding 0.4 mm/sec and 0.2 mm/sec, shall not be discharged into running and attending water bodies respectively.

Floating Impurities

No films, spots of mineral oils or accumulation of other impurities are allowed to float on the surfaces of water bodies.

Odor and Taste

Water should not have odor or smack that can be assessed by more than 2 degrees in untreated water or chlo-untreated water rented water.

Color

Color of water should not be perceived in 20 cm high column, 10 cm high column.

Temperature

The temperature of water in summer should not rise, due to the disposal of sewage, more than 3oC over the mean monthly temperature of water in the hottest month during past ten years.

pH (This Test in Document No. Water- 002-r0)

This should be within the range 6.5-8.5.

Mineral Composition

The dry residue should to agree with require-not exceed 1000 mg/liter; mends do “taste”, (q.v.) the figure includes 350 mg/liter of chlorides and 500 mg/liter of sulphates.

Dissolved Oxygen  (This Test in Document No. Water- 013-r0)

Not less than 4 mg/liter in any season in a sample taken before noon (12 A.M.).

BOD (This Test in Document No. Water- 013-r0)

  1. This test determines the amount of organic material in wastewater by measuring the oxygen consumed by microorganisms in decomposing organic constituents of the waste.
    The test consists of the determination of dissolved oxygen prior to and following 5-day incubation of the sample at 20oC, thus establishing the amount of oxygen used. With wastewater and plant effluents, dilutions of the sample are made with standard dilution water and the dissolved oxygen determined before and after the 5-day incubation period.

  2. The complete oxygen demand of water at 20oC should not exceed 3.0 mg/liter, 6.0 mg/liter.

Pathogenic Causative Agents

Water should be free from pathogenic agents. Sewage which may contain pathogenic causative agents should be disinfected after preliminary treatment.

Biologically treated municipal sewage is disinfected to coli-index not over 1000 in one liter, residual chlorine content being not less than 1.5 mg/liter.

The methods of disinfection and preliminary treatment (mechanical or biological) should be agreed upon with state sanitary inspection in each particular case.

Poisons  

These should not be contained in concentrations which can produce direct or indirect harm to man.
