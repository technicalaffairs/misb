Introduction

Pretreatment of cooling water systems is necessary to maximum the service life of heat exchange equipment. Pretreatment consists of  two phases:

  1. Precleaning- to remove the accumulation of foreign matter that could reduce heat transfer, restrict flow and impair corrosion protection. This procedure prepares the surface for the prefilming phase.

  2. Prefilming- to promote the rapid formation of an inhibiting film over surface of the entire cooling system or critical exchanger depending on the method of application. Prefilming minimizes the initial corrosion which occurs at start-up and allows for the most efficient application of the continuing corrosion inhibitor program. Economics, discharge limitations, and time requirements dictate whether pretreatment is applied to the entire system or to individual exchangers.

Information

Precleaning

All new equipment should be precleaned to remove grease, oil, corrosion products, mill scale, and dirt. This cleaning will provide clean surfaces for promotion of uniform protective film by corrosion inhibitors. Failure to preclean can result in increased corrosion and fouling, resulting in loss of heat transfer, excessive pressure drop across critical heat exchangers, premature failures and high maintenance costs.

Precleaning of the entire system or individual component should be conducted just prior to start-up, followed immediately by rapid passivation of surfaces to avoid initial high corrosion rates.

Normally, a cleaning solution of a polyphosphate, surfactant and antifoam is circulated though the cooling system while the following conditions exist:

  1. The temperature is maintained at 140F 20F (50-70C).

  2. pH is held in the range of 5.5-7.0.

  3. The cleaning is conducted over a period of 8-24 hours.

  4. The polyphosphate concentration is maintained in range of 1-4%.

  5. When the program is completed, the system must be blowndown heavily for rapid removal of the precleaning chemicals.

Laboratory experience has shown that attaining the recommended elevated temperature during pretreatment provides optimum results. In practice it's often difficult to achieved 140F unless individual exchangers are being treated with relatively small solution volumes. Circulating the precleaning solution for the maximum period of time can compensate for lower solution temperature. Industry experience indicates that precleaning even at lower temperature provides significantly improved results over no precleaning at all.

A more easily applied but somewhat less effective precleaning program consists of phosphates, low-molecular-weight polymers, surface active agent and an antifoam. This program can be applied under ambient conditions and at pH range of 6 to 7.  

Prefilming

Most methods of corrosion control are based on forming of a film that acts as a barrier forms will largely determine the effectiveness of treatment. Materials that don't form films rapidly will permit corrosion to take place and the result will be incomplete film formation and continued corrosion. The rate at which the film forms is related to the inhibitor concentration.

The function of prefilming  is to permit rapid formation of a uniform film that immediately stifles the corrosion reaction.

When this film is established, continuous, low treatment levels will maintain the film intact and avoid accumulation of corrosion products. The continuous low treatment level heals slight breaks that may occur from minor variations in environment.

Whenever any serious changes in environment occur that cause destruction of the film, corrosion products can accumulate before the film reestablished low treatment level. Under these conditions normal control should be restored and a prefilming program should be conducted for rapid repassivation of the system.

Prefilming of equipment in cooling water systems is recommended under the following conditions:

  1. For all new heat exchanger equipment.

  2. Whenever cooling water equipment is chemically cleaned.

  3. Whenever low pH excursions are encountered.

  4. Whenever serious process leakage occurs.

  5. Immediately following start up after turnarounds or inspections.

The most prefilming program uses chromate, phosphate and zinc. Generally these materials are circulated through the system in the following concentration ranges:

50 to 300 ppm chromate,

400 to 600 ppm phosphate,

50 to100 ppm zinc.

A protective film of iron, zinc and chromium oxides as well as iron, calcium and zinc phosphate is rapidly formed with the use of these inhibitors.

Prefilming is typically performed from 120F-140F and pH range 6-7 for 4-8 hours. After prefilming is completed the system must be heavily blowndown until normal inhibitor level has been achieved.

Chromate prefilming can be performed by soaking as well as circulation. Individual equipment should be soaked for 24 hours at ambient conditions and pH of 6-7. Highly concentration of inhibitor is required for soaking compared to circulating the fluid.

A non chromate prefilming program using phosphate and zinc has also proven effective in rapidly providing a protective film. This program is maintained from 4-6 hours at pH 6-7 and temperature of 120F.

The system is then heavily blowndown until the phosphate level drops below 20 ppm. At that time the pH should be adjusted until it reaches the level of continuous program.

The third method of prefilming is to increase the normal inhibitor level from 24-48 hours.

This is somewhat easier but less efficient and normally used only when the continuous inhibitor program consists of chromate and zinc.

Directives

Some important precautions concerning precleaning and pretreatment are as follows:

  1. Initial system design should include provisions for isolating critical bundles to facilitate individual pretreatment and to achieve optimum effectiveness and efficiency.

  2. Provisions for air bumping and/or backwashing exchangers should be considered wherever feasible.

  3. Installation of quick-opening blowdown valves on the underside of an exchanger when water is on the shell side is beneficial in minimizing deposit buildup prior to baffles.

  4. Maximum prefilming benefit will be derived only if the system surfaces are clean.

  5. Increasing the temperature promotes rapid film formation.

  6. The application of high chemical concentrations and blowdown rates must be tempered with regard for environmental discharge limits.

  7. High levels of chromate or zinc can adversely affect biological oxidation waste treatment systems.

  8. Prefilming of individual exchangers is sometimes the most economical procedure and may eliminate disposal problems.

  9. Thorough blowdown of precleaning solutions is mandatory before prefilming.

  10. Rapid blowdown and establishment of normal maintenance controls are also very important after the prefilming has been completed.

While it is imperative that pretreatment programs be used for all steel system because of the higher corrosion rates that occur. The principles of pretreatment have application to and will prove beneficial for cooling water systems containing other metals. For example even where admiralty tube heat exchanger are used. The shells and water boxes are typically constructed of steel. The steel components of these units undergo initially high corrosion rates and produce voluminous corrosion products that interfere with proper and complete film formation as well as heat transfer.

The application of the principles of pretreatment for rapid film formation followed by the use of normal treatment levels for film maintenance will prove beneficial in minimizing corrosion of the steel parts of the system. In general improved heat transfer, longer service life and less plant maintenance are the benefits that will be realized.

Special precautions should be taken with new cooling towers which are typically built with wood that has been  pretreated with a copper-based salt. It's imperative that new towers be flushed to leach out copper salts from the wood before circulating any water through the system. Therefore before precleaning a new tower should be thoroughly water-flushed and sumps should be drained and cleaned.
