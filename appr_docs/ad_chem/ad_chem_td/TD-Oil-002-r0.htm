Introduction

The requirements of insulating oil used in circuit breakers, as distinguished from insulating oil used in transformers, are uniquely different. Modern oil circuit breakers require low viscosity and low pour-point oil since a large percentage of them are used outdoors and, in many cases, at low temperatures. All circuit breakers are “free breathing” (open to atmosphere through a breathing device). Which does not prevent the admittance of humid air to the device. In older oil circuit breakers the use of higher viscosity oil is deemed necessary. The resultant effects of oil mixing and the addition of inhibitors upon thermal characteristics must be considered.

Recondition circuit breaker oil is much the same manner as insulating oil; the problem is somewhat different. Whereas sludging may be the principal problem in a transformer. In a circuit breaker the chief function of the insulating oil is to quench the arc, so thermal cracking occurs and minute particles of carbon are formed. These particles, coupled with moisture, can lower the dielectric breakdown voltage of the oil.

Information

Testing

Testing methods for circuit breaker oils are the same as those used for insulating oils. Samples are taken in the same manner as for insulating oil, at specified intervals, varying from a few months to annually. Some of the established ASTM tests applicable to mineral insulating oils are more significant to transformers than to circuit breakers, since a circuit breaker is essentially a free-breathing device that operates at ambient temperature.

New Oil Properties-As Refined

New mineral insulating oils received from the refinery must confirm to certain qualifying functional requirements listed in ASTM D3187-88 [27].

Test Limits: Shipments of New Mineral Insulating Oil

When mineral insulating oil meeting the qualifications of ASTM D3487-88 [27] is received in the field for installation in circuit breakers, it should be checked for certain key values that may be affected by shipment and storage. Viscosity [12 cSt (12 x 10-6 m2/s) maximum at 40оC] and pour point (- 40оC, maximum) should be checked in cold climates to ensure that the oil does not interfere with the free operation of the equipment.

Reconditioning

The chief problem in circuit breaker oil maintenance is to keep the fluid free of water, arc decomposition products, and other contaminants. If visual examination shows the presence of these materials and the dielectric strength of the oil drops below an acceptable value, the oil can be reconditioned, provided it is free of chemical contaminants. The accepted means of reconditioning is by using blotter papers or paper cartridge filters. This subject is discussed in detail in IEEE Std 637-1985 [30].

Directives

New mineral insulating oil must be analyzed before charged into equipment or before energizing the transformer to insure that it is agreed with the recommendations in this guide. If the product standards given by the manufacturer differ from recommendations in this guide the manufacture’s instructions are to be given preference.
