Introduction

Compounds of chlorine are available which have varying degrees of usefulness in treating water and wastewater. These compounds include the hypochlorite of calcium or sodium, chloramines produced by reacting chlorine with ammonia, chlorine dioxide, and chlorine donor complexes such as chlorinated cyanurates.

A number of reactions can occur when chlorine is added to natural waters. Some of the most prominent chlorine applications are:

1.     Disinfection.

2.     Removal of ammonia and other nitrogenous organics.

3.     Control of taste and odor.

4.     Hydrogen sulfide oxidation.

5.     Iron and manganese oxidation.

6.     Destruction of organic matter.

7.     Color reduction.

8.     Control of slime and algae.

9.     Control of iron, manganese and sulfate-reducing bacteria.

Principles

In chemically pure water, elemental chlorine reacts with the water and hydrolyzes to hypochlorous acid (HOCI) and hydrochloric acid (HCL):

                                     Cl2   + H2O       =       HOCL  +   HCL                          

                              Chlorine  + Water   =  Hypochlorous Acid + Hydrochloric Acid                                                                                                       

                                                   HOCL   =  H+ +    OCl -

                                         Hypochlorous  =  hydrogen  +   hypochlorite

                                                Acid                Ion                 Ion

The first reaction is almost instantaneous, and for all practical purposes completely irreversible.

The second reaction, the dissociation of hypochlorous acid to hypochlorite ions (OCl-) and hydrogen ions (H+), is reversible and the equilibrium condition depends on pH and temperature. Because the dissociation is so rapid, the equilibrium ratio at any given pH remains constant even though the hypochlorous acid concentration may be decreasing (see figure1).

The prime oxidizing agents in water are hypochlorous acid, and to a much lesser degree, hypochlorite ions. Any unreacted excess of hypochlorous acid and hypochlorite ion is classified as free available residual chlorine.

Definations

The oxidation-reduction potential is a measure of the tendency of chlorine to react with other materials, is not a measure of the speed with which these reactions occur. As the pH increases, the chemical reactivity of chlorine decreases; as temperature increases, reactions proceed more rapidly.

The oxidation reactions of chlorine with such inorganic reducing agents as sulfides, sulfites and nitrites are generally very rapid. Some dissolved organic materials also react rapidly with chlorine, but the completion of many organic-chlorine reactions may take hours.

Chlorine demand is defined as the difference between the amount of chlorine applied to a water and the amount of free combined or total available chlorine which remains after a specific contact time.

Breakpoint chlorination is the addition of chlorine to water until the chlorine demand has been satisfied, and further additions produce a residual chlorine which is directly proportional to the amount added beyond the breakpoint.

Residual chlorine is the total amount of free and combined chlorine remaining in water after the chlorine demand has been satisfied. Free residual chlorination is the application of chlorine to water to produce a free available chlorine residual. Free available residual chlorine is the residual chlorine which exists in water as hypochlorous acid and hypochlorite ions.

Combined residual chlorination is the application of chlorine to water to produce with natural or added ammonia, a combined available chlorine residual. Combined available residual chlorine is the chlorine residual which exists in water in chemical combination with ammonia or other organic nitrogen compounds (chloramines) after the water’s chlorine demand has been satisfied.

Pre-chlorination is the application of chlorine to water prior to other treatment processes. Re-chlorination is the application of chlorine at one or more points of a system to water which has already been chlorinated. Post-chlorination is the application of chlorine to a water effluent from other treatment processes. De-chlorination is partially or completely reducing residual chlorine by chemical or physical means.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Disinfection

Disinfecting water implies the destructive deactivation of pathogenic and other organisms. This does not necessarily mean sterilization, nor the

destruction of all living organisms. Sterilization is largely confined to medical and other specialized industrial applications.

The bactericidal action of chlorine is considered to be physiochemical, as a result of the rapid oxidizing power of hypochlorous acid.

However the events surrounding the killing are obscure, theoretically, bacterical cells are destroyed by the irreversible chemical reaction of chlorine with a host of cellular components. This would explain the extreme sensitivity of some organisms to chlorine, but fails to account for the differences in sensitivity manifested by a variety of microbial types. It is not known by what mechanism certain forms can withstand chlorine concentrations well above those lethal for other forms.

Hypochlorous acid reacting with ammonia, ammonia salts or amines to form chloramines will reduce the disinfecting ability of chlorine.

The formation of chloramines follows these reactions:

                                   HOCL     +        NH3              =         NH2CL  +  H2O

                    Hypochlorous Acid  +   Ammonia   =     Monochloramine  + Water

                                   2HOCL   +    NH3         =     NHCL2   +   2H2O

                   Hypochlorous Acid  +  Ammonia    = Dichloramine +  Water

The pH of the treated water determines which product will be formed. At pH 8.5 monochloramine is the major product, at about 4.5 dichloramine is the major product, between these pH value  mixtures of the two prevail and below pH 4.5  nitrogen trichloride is the predominant reaction product.

Where chloramines occur, a longer exposure time or an increased chlorine concentration is necessary to achieve comparable bacterial reduction. The oxidizing properties of chlorine are substantially lower in such combinations and the reaction of chlorine with organic matter is retarded. This helps to maintain a stable residual in outlying water distribution system. Although this combined residual is ordinarily more stable than a free chlorine residual, its lower bactericidal qualities require maintaining higher residuals.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Breakpoint Chlorination

Normal chlorination of water is generally described as marginal. Very simply, breakpoint chlorination is the application of enough chlorine, after the chlorine demand has been satisfied, to maintain free available chlorine residuals. add mg chlorine to water which contains ammonia or nitrogen-containing organic matter will first produce an increased combined chlorine residual.

After the maximum residual is reached, further chlorine doses will decrease the residual values because the combined available chlorine residuals formed when the chlorine was first added, will decompose. Decrease of the chlorine residual will not being until the additional chlorine is at least eight or more times the amount of free ammonia present. If this chlorine-ammonia ratio is less than 8:1.for example 4:1 chloramines will form, and the breakpoint reactions will not occur. When this instantaneous chlorine demand is high, the chlorine requirements needed to provide free available chlorine residuals may be 20 or more times the quantity of ammonia present.

Hypochlorination

The most common commercial forms of hypochlorite include sodium hypochlorite with about 15 % available chlorine, and high test calcium hypochlorite with 70% available chlorine. The hypochlorite salt most widely used to treat industrial waters is calcium hypochlorite,Ca(OCI)2. When this material dissolves in water, ionization occurs:

                    Ca(OCI)2                 =       Ca--          +     2(OCI)-

             Calcium   Hypochlorite         =  Calcium Ions  +  Hypochlorite  Ions

The hypochlorite ions establish an equilibrium with hydrogen ions, depending on pH, as does gaseous chlorine. The effect of pH is significant. Solutions of hypochlorite contain excess alkali which tends to raise the pH. And in some hard waters, calcium carbonate precipitates to cause scale problems. Usually, adding a complex phosphate will overcome this difficulty. In other cases, the high alkalinity of the hypochlorite treatment improves the quality of soft and highly corrosive waters.

Although calcium hypochlorite is soluble in water, when it dissolves in hard water, it often produces a precipitate. To feed calcium hypochlorite as a liquid, solutions should be prepared at approximately 1-2 % available chlorine.

Sodium hypochlorite, NaOCl, is available in several forms, ranging from the familiar grocery store bleaches at a concentration of about 5 % , to commercial laundry bleaches at a concentration of 12-15%. Most are quite unstable.

Sodium hypochlorite may be fed satisfactorily in the normal 15% available chlorine strength or diluted with water. All hypochlorites are somewhat harmful to skin and must be handled carefully. Satisfactory corrosion–resistant materials should be used for strong and dispensing hypochlorites.

De-Chlorination

Activated carbon or such chemical reducing agents as sulfur dioxide, sodium  sulfite, sodium bisulfite or sodium thiosulfate will dechlorinate water, but may also promote the growth of bacteria which use the sulfur forms. Prolonged storage in open reservoirs will also de-chlorinate water effectively through the action of ultraviolet radiation in sunlight.

De-chlorination may be desirable for either public or industrial water supplies. Reducing or removing the chlorinous taste from public water may be necessary. High chlorine residuals which adversely affect many industrial processes must be removed or reduced. Also, residual chlorine adversely affects some resins used in ion exchange softeners and demineralizers. Consequently, de-chlorination is required ahead of these units.

Other Uses and Effects of Chlorine

In addition to serving as bactericides, chlorine and chlorine compounds are used to reduce objectionable tastes and odors from drinking water; oxidize iron, manganese and hydrogen sulfide and aid in their removal, and oxidize, de-color and destroy organic matter.

Chlorine, injected into sewage and industrial wastes before they are discharged to the receiving stream. will reduce bacteria and destroy such chemicals as sulfides, sulfites and ferrous ion which may react directly with oxygen. Chlorine is widely used in once-through and recirculating cooling water systems to control slime and algae.

Chlorine, a long with a coagulant, is often applied to the raw water in clarification processes. Such pre-chlorination improves coagulation because of the action of chlorine on the organic material in the water. It also reduces tastes, odor and color-production materials by oxidizing them, reduces biological loading, and oxidizes iron and manganese to facilitate removal by settling and filtration.

Chlorine is a successful activating agent for sodium silicate, in the preparation of the coagulant aid, activated silica. This process has the advantage that the chlorine used for the activation is available for other purposes.

Many process water are chlorinated to control bacteria and slime-producing organisms. Direct contact of water containing a chlorine residual with food products is avoided in the food industries because of possible taste effects in canned or frozen foods. Before using chlorinated water in food processing, de-chlorination is general practice. Also, excessive chlorine affects the smoothness and brightness of materials in the plating industry.

Paper mills, which produce fine paper, usually establish very critical limits for chlorine residuals.

The concentration of chlorine required to control plankton and algae in reservoirs varies from 0.2 to 3.0 ppm, depending on the sensitivity and type of organisms present. Concentrations as great as 50 ppm may be needed to destroy some types. Free available chlorine in water is toxic to fish and other aquatic life at low concentrations. Combined  with cyanide phenols and other substances, chlorine may be even more toxic to aquatic forms of life.

Little specific data is available to define the limits of physiological tolerance for chlorine in potable water. But it is generally agreed that as soon as water is swallowed, the small amounts of chlorine present are destroyed by reaction with saliva and gastric juices.

Feeding Equipment

Chlorination equipment is commercially available for both liquefied chlorine gas and solutions of sodium hypochlorite. Calcium hypochlorite is a powder and is generally added by shot feeding.

Liquid chlorine is the least expensive form of chlorine and is generally used except on smaller applications. Because of the hazards of a chlorine leak, feeding equipment is designed to reduce chlorine gas pressure to below atmospheric as soon as possible. Such operation causes any leaks to be into the feeding system rather than out to the surrounding atmosphere (see figure 4).

Depending on the quantity required, sodium hypochlorite feeders are either metering pumps or flow-controlled rotameter indicators. Because sodium hypochlorite solutions contain both chlorine and some excess caustic soda, the solutions are both corrosive and highly alkaline. Although less dangerous than chlorine gas, hypochlorite solutions require very carefully selected feeding equipment.
