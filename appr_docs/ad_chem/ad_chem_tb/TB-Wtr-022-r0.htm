Introduction

All natural waters contain, in various concentrations, dissolved salts which dissociate in water to form charged  particles called "ions", the positively charged called "cations" and  the negatively charged called "anions".

Calcium, magnesium, and sodium along with bicarbonate, sulfate and chloride anions are most present in raw water.

In ion–exchanger, ionic concentrations are expressed as "p.p.m Calcium Carbonate" with the exceptions of carbon dioxide (CO2) and silica (SiO2) total cations exactly equal total anions.

Information

Ion Exchanger  

They are materials that exchange one ion for another, hold it temporarily, and release it to a regenerant solution. These compounds are widely used to treat raw water supplies which contain dissolved salts. A proper regenerate can replace undesirable ions into water supply with other more acceptable ions, for example the commonly used "Sodium Zeolite Softener" replaces scale forming calcium and magnesium ions with sodium ions.

Classification of Ion Exchange Resins

 Ionizable groups attached to the resin bead determine  the functional capability of the resin. Present industrial water  treatment resins may be classified into four basic types.  

  • Strong acid cation – SAC–

  • Weak acid cation  –WAC–

  • Strong base anion  – SBA–

  • Weak base anion   –WBA–

 

The strong acid cation (SAC) and the strong base anion (SBA) resins can convert neutral salts into their corresponding acids and bases, respectively. This ability, called salt splitting, results in different treated water quality than that obtained by treatment with (WAC) or (WBA) resins.

(SAC) resins derive their exchange activity from sulfonic acid function groups ( HSO3-).

Ca          2HCO3                                                   Ca               2H2CO3

Mg         SO4          +  2 Z SO3H  ↔   2 Z SO3     Mg       +     H2SO4

2Na        2Cl                                                         2Na             2HCl

 

Where "Z"  represents the complex resin matrix because  these reactions are reversible. When the resin capacity has been exhausted it can be recovered through regeneration  with a mineral acid.

Strong acid cation  (SAC) exchangers  function well at all pH ranges and can split neutral salts into their corresponding acids. These resins have found a wide range of applications, being used on the sodium cycle for softening and in hydrogen cycle for softening and decationization.

Weak acid cation (WAC) exchange  resins having carboxylic groups (-COOH) as an exchange sites when operated in the hydrogen cycle, (WAC) resins can remove cations associated with alkalinity according to

Ca                                                                    Ca                   

Mg     (HCO3)2   +  2 ZCOOH  ↔ 2 ZCOO   Mg   +   2H2CO3  

2Na                                                                  2Na            

These reactions are also reversible and permit acid regeneration to return the exhausted resin to the hydrogen form. The main asset of the (WAC) resins in their high regeneration efficiency which not only reduce the amount of acid required for regeneration, but also minimizes waste disposal problems.

WAC resins are used primarily for softening and dealkalization, frequently in conjunction with strongly acidic polishing resin. Systems which use both resins refit from the regeneration  economy of the weakly acidic resin while producing treated water of quality comparable to that available with strongly acidic only.

Strong base anion (SBA) resins derive their functionality from  quaternary ammonium exchange sites. Two species of quaternary ammonium exchange sites are commercially available and are commonly referred to as Type  I & II.

Type I  sites have three methyl groups

 

 

                          CH3

                      |   

                        (R – N –CH3)+

                    |

                          CH3

 

 

Type II an ethanol group replace one of the methyl groups

                                     CH2CH2OH

                      |   

                          (R – N –CH3)+

                    |

                          CH3

            

The principal difference between the two resins, operationally is that type I resin has a greater chemical stability and is able to remove more of the weakly ionized acids. Type II resin has a slightly greater regeneration efficiency and a greater capacity for the same amount of regenerant chemical used.

In the hydroxide form (SBA) resins  remove all commonly encountered anions (inorganic acids) according to these reactions.

H2SO4                                                                       SO4

2HCl                                                            2Cl     

2H2SiO3    + 2 Z.OH    <----------->   2 Z.  2HSiO3   + 2 H2O

2H2CO3                                                                    2HCO3

Like the cation resins, these reactions are reversible, allowing regeneration with a strong alkali, such as caustic soda, to return the resin to the hydroxide form.

Weak base anion (WBA) resins derive their functionality from primary (R–NH2) amine groups, (WBA)  resins readily absorbs such free mineral acids as hydrochloric, sulfuric and nitric and the reaction may be represented as

H2SO4                                                              SO4

2HCl     + 2 Z.OH    <----------->   2 Z. 2Cl    +  2 H2O 

2HNO3                                                 NO3   

(WBA) resin are essentially mineral acid neutralizers. Because the above reactions are also reversible. (WBA) resins can be regenerated by applying caustic soda, soda ash or ammonia. Regeneration efficiency of (WBA) resins is substantial greater than for strong base anion resins. While (WBA) resins readily adsorb free mineral acids, these resins won't remove such weakly ionized as silicic and carbonic acids.

Weakly basic anion resins are used in demineralizer systems in conjunction with strongly basic resins to reduce regenerant costs to scavenge organics thereby protecting the more susceptible strongly basic resins and for service in which carbon dioxide and silica are not important.

 

 

 

Principles of Demineralization

The demineralization system consists of one or more ion exchange resin columns, which include a strong acid cation (SAC) unit and a strong base anion (SBA) unit.

 

The cation resin exchanges hydrogen for raw water cations by these reactions.

Ca          SO4                                                 Ca                  H2SO4

Mg    .    2Cl           + 2Z .H → 2Z .  Mg     +        2HCl

2Na       2HCO3                                         2Na              2H2CO3

               2NO3                                                           2HNO3

Where Z cation exchange resin complex.

The anion resin  exchanges hydroxyl anions for the highly ionized anions as shown by these reaction.

H2SO4                                                               SO4

2HCL     + 2 Z. OH  <-----------> 2 Z  . 2CL     + 2 H2O

2HNO3                                                              2NO3

Where Z represents the anion exchange resin complex. Because the anion used as demineralizer resin is strongly basic, it will remove weakly ionized carbonic and silicic acids, according  to these reactions:

2 H2CO3                                                                2 HCO3

2 H2SiO3   + 2 Z. OH  <-----------> 2 Z .  2 HSiO3   + 2 H2O

The above reactions indicate complete removal of cations and anions from the water, but in reality, these are equilibrium reactions and even with very efficient column operation, leakage will occur. The leakage will vary according to the demineralizer system used and for any given system according to the raw water mineral composition.

This insures that the water will pass through the most  highly regenerated resin just before leaving the unit.

Demineralization systems for producing water of extremely high purity use mixed bed demineralizer an intimate mixture of strongly acidic cation and strongly basic anion resins in the same column.

A typical mixed bed demineralizer will rinse down to low conductivity and silica values after regeneration.

These values will remain low and really constant during the service cycle. But when the resin is exhausted, both  value will increase concurrently.

Treated water quality will decrease when resin capacity is exceeded, thus indicating the need to remove the unit from service for regeneration. The cation exchange resin  is regenerated with acid as described for hydrogen zeolite unit. The anion exchange resin is regenerated with alkaline material. Caustic soda is the most common regenerant. Usually applied four percent solution (4%). Regenerates the resin in accordance with these reaction:

          SO4                                                                                    Na2SO4

2 Z    2Cl           + 2NaOH  <----------->   2ZOH  +     2NaCl

          2NO3                                                                     2NaNO3

          2HCO3                                                                           2NaHCO3

          2HSiO3                                                                 2NaHSiO3

At the end of a strong base anion unit service run, silica will increase, pH will decrease and conductivity will temporarily decrease followed by an increase.
