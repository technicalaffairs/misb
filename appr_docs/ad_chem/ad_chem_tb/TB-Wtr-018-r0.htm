Introduction

Suspended matter in raw water supplies is removed by various methods to provide a water suitable for domestic purposes and most industrial requirements. Removal is generally accomplished by coagulation, flocculation and sedimentation. The combination of these three processes is  referred to as conventional clarification.

Coagulation is the process of "destabilization by charge neutralization". Once neutralized,  particles no longer repel each other and can be brought together. Coagulation is necessary for the removal  of the colloidal-sized suspended matter.

Flocculation is the process of bringing together the destabilized or "coagulated" particles to form a larger agglomeration or "floc".

Sedimentation refers to the physical removal from suspension, or settling, that occurs once the particles have been coagulated and flocculated. Sedimentation or subsidence alone, without prior coagulation results in the removal of  relatively coarse suspended solids only.

Information

Steps of Clarification

Finely divided particles suspended in surface water repel each other because their surfaces are predominantly negatively charged.

Clarification involves the following steps to allow the particles to agglomerate:

Coagulation

  1. Use of inorganic salts of aluminum or iron which neutralize the charge on the turbidity-particle. These also hydrolyze to form insoluble precipitates, which entrap additional particles.

  2. Use of a water-soluble organic polymers with numerous 'ionized sites for particle charge neutralization.

 

 

Flocculation

Flocculation, the agglomeration of destabilized particles into large particles, can be enhanced by the addition of high molecular-weight, water-soluble organic polymers, which increase floc size by charged site binding and by molecular bridging.

Inorganic Coagulants

Table1- lists a number of common inorganic coagulants. Except for sodium aluminate, all common iron and aluminum coagulants are acid salts which lower the pH of the treated water by hydrolysis. Depending on initial raw water alkalinity and pH, an alkali such as lime or caustic must be added to counteract the pH depression of the primary or inorganic coagulant.

Because pH can affect both particle surface charge and floc precipitation during coagulation, it is an extremely important variable. Iron and aluminum hydroxide flocs are best precipitated at pH levels which minimize the hydroxide solubility. However, the best pH for coagulating suspended solids does not always coincide with the optimum pH for minimum hydroxide floc solubility.

With aluminum sulfate, the optimum coagulation efficiency and minimum floc solubility normally occur at pH 6.0 to 7.0. Sodium aluminate, being alkaline is best used at elevated pH's, such as are encountered in precipitation softening. Iron coagulants can be used successfully over the much broader pH range of 5.0 to 11.0. But when ferrous compounds are used, oxidation to ferric iron is needed for complete precipitation. This may require either chlorination or pH levels greater than 8.0.

 Polyelectrolytes

The term polyelectrolyte refers to all water-soluble organic polymers used for clarification, whether they function as primary coagulants or flocculants.

Water-soluble polymers available may be classified as follows:

  1. Anionic-ionize in water solution to form negative sites along the polymer molecule,

  2. Cationic-ionize to form positive sites,

  3. Nonionic-ionize in water to form very slight negatively charged sites along the polymer chain.

Polymeric primary coagulants are cationic materials with relatively low molecular-weights (less than 500,000). The cationic charge density (available positively charged sites) is very high. Polymeric flocculants or coagulant aids may be anionic, cationic, or nonionic. Their molecular weights will range up to 50,000,000. They function primarily by bridging particles together.

For any given particle there is an ideal molecular weight and an ideal charge density for optimum coagulation. Further, there is an optimum charge and molecular weight for the most efficient flocculants.

Since suspensions are normally nonuniform, specific testing is necessary to find the coagulants and flocculants with the broadest range of performance.

Primary Coagulant Polyelectrolytes

Cationic polyelectrolyte commonly used as primary coagulants are polyamines. They exhibit strong cationic ionization and have relatively low molecular weights of less than 500,000. When used as primary coagulants, they adsorb on particle surfaces, thus neutralizing the repelling negative charges. The polymer may also bridge, for  some extent, from one particle to another. This accomplishes clarification and flocculation without the requirement for precipitation of additional suspended solids by inorganic coagulants. The pH of the treated water is unaffected.

The efficiency of primary coagulant polyelectrolytes depends greatly on the exact nature of the turbidity particles to be coagulated, the amount of turbidity present, and the turbulence available during coagulation. With lower influent turbidities, more turbulence or mixing is required to achieve maximum charge neutralizations.

Raw waters of less than 10 Nephelometric Turbidity Units (NTU) usually cannot be clarified with a cationic polymer alone. Best results are obtained by a "combination of an inorganic salt and cationic polymer".

With greater than 60 NTU turbidity, the use of a primary coagulant polymer alone is normally sufficient.

Another treatment approach can be used on low-turbidity waters where it is desirable to avoid using an inorganic coagulant, artificial turbidity in the form of  bentonite clay can be added to build floc. The addition of a clay provides additional surface area for adsorption and entrapment of finely divided turbidity. A polymeric coagulant is added to complete the coagulation process.

 

A few of the advantages of the organic polymer-type coagulants are:

  1. The volume of sludge produced during clarification can be reduced by 50 to 90%.

  2. The resulting sludge contains less water and can be more easily dewatered.

  3. Polymeric coagulants do not affect pH. The need for an alkaline chemical such as lime, caustic or soda ash is thereby reduced or eliminated.

  4. Polymeric coagulants do not add to the total dissolved solids concentration. For example, alum adds 0.45 ppm of sulfate ion (as CaCO3) for each ppm of commercial alum used. The reduction in sulfate may significantly extend the capacity of anion exchange systems.

  5. Soluble iron or aluminum carryover in the clarifier effluent may result from inorganic coagulant use. Therefore, elimination of the inorganic coagulant can minimize the deposition of these metals in filters, ion exchange units, and cooling systems.

  

 

Coagulant Aids (Flocculants)

In certain instances, an excess of primary coagulant, whether inorganic, polymeric or a combination of both is fed to promote large floc  size and settling rate. A polymeric coagulant aid added after the primary coagulant can, by developing a dense floc at low treatment levels, reduce the amount of primary coagulant required.

Coagulant aids also broaden the pH range over which satisfactory; coagulation may occur. In-Addition they can be-used to eliminate floc carryover from the sedimentation unit. Generally, very high molecular weight, slight anionic polyacrylamides are the most effective coagulant aids. Nonionic or cationic types have proven successful in some clarifier systems. Essentially, the polymer bridges the small floc particles and causes them to agglomerate rapidly into bigger and more cohesive flocs which settles quickly.

Color Reduction

Frequently, the objective of clarification is the reduction of color. Swamps and wetlands introduce color into surface waters, particularly after heavy rainfalls. Color can cause various problems such as objectionable taste, increased bacteria and algae growth, fouling of anion exchange resins and  interference with coagulation and stabilization of soluble iron and manganese.

Most organic color in surface waters is colloidal and negatively charged. Chemically, color-producing compounds are classified as humic and fulvic acids. Color can be removed by chlorination and coagulation with aluminum or iron salts. Chlorine will oxidize color compounds while the inorganic coagulants can physically remove many types of organic color by neutralization of surface charges. Further color removal is obtained by chemical interaction with aluminum or iron hydrolysis products. Cationic organic polyelectrolytes can also coagulate some types of color particles

Coagulation for color reduction is normally carried out at pH 4.5 to 5.5. Optimum pH for turbidity removal is usually much higher than that for color reduction. The sulfate ions can interfere with coagulation for color reduction, whereas calcium and magnesium ions can improve the process and broaden the pH range in which color may be reduced effectively.

Conventional Clarification Equipment

The coagulation, flocculation and sedimentation process requires three distinct unit processes;

  1. Rapid mix for coagulation.

  2. Moderate mixing for flocculation.

  3. Floc and water separation.

Horizontal Flow Clarifiers

Originally, conventional clarification units consisted of large, rectangular, concrete basins divided into two or three sections. Each stage of the clarification process occurred in one section of the basin. Water flow was horizontal through such systems.

Horizontal flow units are still used in very large industrial plants and for clarifying municipal water since the design is suited to large capacity basins. The retention time is normally long, (up to 4 to 6 hr), and is chiefly devoted to settling. Rapid mix is typically designed for 3 to 5 minutes and slow mix for 15 to 30 minutes. This design affords great  flexibility in establishing proper chemical addition points. Also, such units are relatively insensitive to sudden changes in water throughput.

Likewise, the long retention allows sufficient reaction time to make necessary adjustments in chemical and polymer feed if raw water conditions suddenly change. However, for all but very large treated water demands, horizontal units require high construction costs and more land space per unit of water capacity.

Upflow Clarifiers

Compact and relatively economical, upflow clarifiers accommodate coagulation, flocculation and sedimentation in a single (usually circular) steel or concrete tank. These clarifiers are termed "upflow" because the water flows up while the suspended solids settle. A key feature in maintaining a high-clarity effluent is increased solids concrete through internal sludge recirculation.

Because retention time in an upflow unit is approximately 1 to 2 hr, upflow basins can be much smaller in size or holding capacity than horizontal basins of equal throughput capacity. A rise rate of 0.70 to 1.25 gpm/ ft2 of surface area is normal for clarification. Combination softening-clarification units may operate up to 1.5 gpm/ft2 per of surface area due to particle size and densities of precipitated hardness

The throughput efficiency of upflow units is achieved by maximizing the linear overflow weir length while minimizing the opportunity for short-circuiting through the settling zone. In addition, the two mixing stages for coagulation and flocculation occur within the same clarification  tank.

Although upflow units may provide more efficient sedimentation than horizontal designs, many upflow clarifiers compromise on the rapid and slow mix sequences. Some types provide rapid, mechanical mixing and rely on flow turbulence for flocculation; others eliminate the rapid mix stage and provide only moderate turbulence for flocculation. However, in most cases, rapid mix deficiencies can be over-come by adding the primary coagulant farther upstream of the clarifier.

Sludge Blanket and Solids-Contact Clarification

Most upflow designs are called either "sludge blanket" or "solids-contact" clarifiers. After coagulation and/or flocculation in the sludge blanket units, the incoming water passes through the suspended layer of previously formed floc. Figure (2) shows an upflow sludge blanket clarifier.

Because the center in these units is often shaped like an inverted cone, the rise rate of  the water decreases as it rises through the steadily enlarging cross section. When the rise rate decreases enough to exactly equal the settling rate of the suspended floc, a distinct sludge/liquid interface will form.

Sludge blanket efficiency depends on the filtering action as the freshly coagulated or flocculated water passes through the suspended floc. Higher sludge levels increase the filtration efficiency. In practice, the top sludge interface is carried it in highest safe level to avoid upsets which might result in large  amounts of floc carryover into the overflow. Likewise, excessive sludge withdrawal or blowdown should be avoided. The sludge blanket level is often highly sensitive to changes in throughoutput, coagulant addition and changes in raw water chemistry and temperature.

Solids-contact refers to units in which large volumes of sludge are circulated internally. The term solids-contact also describes the sludge blanket unit and simply means that prior to and during sedimentation the chemically treated water contacts previously coagulated solids. Solids-contact units often combine both clarification and precipitation softening. Bringing the incoming raw water into contact with recirculated sludge improves the efficiency of the softening reactions, and increases the size and density of the floc particles.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

In-Line Clarification

In-line clarification is the process of raw water turbidity removal by coagulant addition just prior to filtration. Polyelectrolytes and/or inorganic coagulants are fed to improve filtration efficiency and run length. Polymers are favored since they do not add additional suspended solids loading which would necessarily decrease run length.

Equipment design may be downflow or upflow, depending on raw water turbidity and particle size. The downflow dual-media unit generally consists of layers of various grades of anthracite and sand supported on a gravel bed. After backwashing, the larger anthracite particles separate to the top of the bed, while the more dense, smaller particles are at the bottom. The purpose is to allow bed penetration of the floc, which reduces the potential for excessive pressure drops due to blinding off the top portion of filter media. Thus, higher filtration rates are realized without a significant loss in effluent quality due to the feature of increased bed use. Normal filtration rates are 5 to 6 gpm/ft2.

Coagulant Selection and Feeding for In-Line Clarification

The choice of a polymer coagulant and feed rate depends on equipment design and influent water turbidity. Initially, in-line clarification was used in the treatment of low turbidity waters, but it is now being utilized on many types of surface waters. On most waters, the use of a cationic polymer coagulant alone is satisfactory. However, the addition of a high-molecular-weight, cationic acrylamide copolymer may improve filtration efficiency.

Polymer feed rates (ppm) are usually less than those used in conventional clarification given the same raw water characteristics. Complete charge neutralization and bridging is not necessary and should be avoided. Total coagulation or flocculation may promote excessive entrapment of suspended solids in the first portion of the filter media. This can cause blinding over of the media, high pressure drops and short operating runs.

Polymer is applied only to  provide sufficient neutralization to allow entire-bed often and adsorption of particles through the entire bed. Often, polymer feed rates are regulated by trial and error on the actual units to minimize effluent turbidity and maximize service run length.  Since optimum flocculation is undesirable, polymer are injected just upstream of the units. Normally, a short mixing period is required to achieve the degree of reaction most suitable for unit operation. Dilution water may lie recommended to disperse the polymer throughout the incoming water. However, it may lie necessary to move the polymer injection point several times to improve turbidity removal. Due to the nature of operation, a change of polymer feed rate will typically show a change in effluent turbidity in relatively short periods of time.     

Coagulation Testing

Raw water analyses alone are not very useful to predict coagulation conditions. Coagulation chemicals and appropriate dosages must be selected either by operating experience with a given raw water or by simulation or the clarification step on a laboratory scale.

Jar testing best simulates clarification chemistry and operation. A multiple-paddle, beaker arrangement permits comparing various chemical combinations, all of  which are subjected to identical hydraulic conditions. The effects of rapid and slow mix intensity and duration may also be observed. Besides determining the optimum chemical program, the correct order of addition may be established. The most critical measurements in the jar test are coagulant and or flocculants dosages, pH, floc size and settling characteristics, floc-forming time and finished water clarity. The effect of sludge recirculation may be simulated by adding the sludge formed in one series of jar tests to the next jar test. Results of jar tests are only relative and frequent adjustments are necessary in full-scale plant operation.

 

 

Zeta potential measurements have been used experimentally to predict coagulant requirements and optimum pH levels. Because the measurement  require special  apparatus and a skilled technician, zeta potential has never become practical for controlling industrial water clarification plants.

Chemical Additions

Although the most efficient sequence for adding coagulation chemicals will vary depending on the type of water and system used and must be checked by jar testing, it will usually be:

  1. Chlorine,

  2. Bentonite (for low turbidity waters),

  3. Primary inorganic and /or polymer coagulant,

  4. pH-adjusting chemicals,

  5. Coagulant aid.

Chlorine may assist coagulation by oxidizing organic contaminants which have dispersing properties, some waters having high organic content will require increased primary coagulant demand. Chlorination prior to primary coagulant will also reduce the coagulant dosage. When using inorganic coagulant, adding pH-adjusting chemicals prior to the coagulant will establish the proper pH environment for the primary coagulant. 

All treatment chemicals (except coagulant aids should be added during very turbulent mixing of the influent water. Rapid mixing while the aluminum and iron coagulants are added ensures uniform action adsorption onto the suspended matter.

High shear mixing is especially important when using cationic polymers as primary coagulants In general, it is advisable to feed them as far ahead of the clarifier as possible. However, high shear mixing must be avoided when a coagulant aid is added to prevent interference with the polymer's bridging function. Only moderate turbulence is needed to obtain floc growth.
