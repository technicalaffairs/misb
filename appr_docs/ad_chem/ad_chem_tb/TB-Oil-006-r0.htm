Introduction

This document covers the problems  associated with use of heavy fuels which can be substantially reduced or eliminated by chemical modification of these fuels through incorporation of fireside additives. Fireside additives can improve power plant performance and extend equipment life.

Fireside additives are gaining wider recognization in combating problems related to boiler performance and reliability mainly corrosion, fouling, superheat control, acidic emissions and fly ash control.

Information

The problems-causing contaminates most often found in heavy fuels are listed in Table1. They compromise compounds of metals, especially vanadium, sodium, lead, potassium, and the non-metal, sulfur. When fuels containing these elements are burned, mixtures of oxides and sulfates are formed, collectively comprising fuel ash.

Fireside problems can arise in three major areas of a boiler as shown in figure 1. In the furnace area and convection section (superheater and reheater), where temperature exceed 500ºC (~900ºF) deposits due to vanadium, sodium, lead, and potassium, can accumulate and reduce heat transfer. Corrosion of metal tubes and invasion of refractories can also occur if the deposits are in the molten or semi molten state. In the colder areas of the boiler (air preheater and stack) where temperatures may fall below the acid dew point

(below 150ºC /300ºF) sulfuric acid formed from sulfur in the fuel can condense on metal surfaces and destroy them through acidic attack. Another problem area that arise in a heavy-fueled boiler operation is smoking and soot emission, due to incomplete combustion. Fuel additives are provides specialized oil-soluble additives to combat each of these problems. In addition, supplies additives to solve fuel handling problems-cold flow improves, pour point depressants and dispersants.

High Temperature Corrosion and Deposits

When a fuel containing vanadium and sodium are burned in a boiler, ash components consisting of low-melting vanadium and sodium oxides and sulfates are formed. Table 2 shows many of the possible ash materials that can be formed in combustion of contaminated fuels. Although vanadium alone can be a severe problem in causing deposits (V2O5 melts at 675ºC/1247ºF), this temperature easily reached in the superheater and reheater sections of boiler. At superheater temperatures, these ash components begin to soften and impact and adhere to hot boiler surfaces, causing heavy tenacious deposits which impede the free flow of gases in the boiler and reduce heat transfer. This, in turn, causes a drop in steam temperatures and a loss of steam production. If also forces the boiler operator to increase fuel consumption in order to maintain design steam temperatures and pressures.

If the melting point of fuel ash is low enough, semi-molten or molten deposits can attack the metal surfaces (superheater and reheater tubes and hangers). This represents vanadic corrosion or high temperature corrosion in a boiler.

Two methods may be effective for controlling heavy ash deposits (slag) and high temperature corrosion.

  1. Reduce excess flue gas oxygen to the point where lower-valence, higher-melting ash components are formed; but this goal is usually difficult to achieve in a boiler.

  2. Using of a fuel oil additives capable of serving two functions.

    1- Increase the fusion point of the ash components to a point above the prevailing temperatures in the superheater and reheater areas of the boiler. 

    2- Modify the ash that form friable form that won't result in hard deposits and which can be easily removed by normal soot-blowing or (upon shutdown) by water-washing (not high-pressure hydroplasting).

Choice of Inhibitor Metals

Magnesium

Magnesium is used to increase fusion point temperatures of boiler ash. Other alkaline earth-metal additives could be used, but most have disadvantages; e.g. Barium and Calcium form very hard-to-remove deposits. If Magnesium is incorporated in the fuel in a sufficiently available and active form, it will react in the flame with Vanadium oxides to form higher melting Magnesium vanadates, some of which are shown in Table 3. This vanadates melt at temperature well above those normally occuring in the convection  sections of boilers.

Aluminum

When the Sodium content [or the (Na+K)/V ratio] of vanadic fuels becomes substantial, control of deposits and corrosion by Magnesium additives may falter, due to a Sodium fluxing effect (see Table 2). We have found that a specific element, Aluminum, is needed for Sodium control. Additives containing Alumina (Al2O3) due to its chemical passivity, its major effect is a physical one: ash friability may be improved (via dilution) when large amounts of  (Al2O3) are employed. For Aluminum to be effective in controlling the fluxing effect of Sodium in combustion ash, it must be available for chemical reaction. Specifically, to invoke the high melting point effect of Aluminum on the Sodium content of fuels, a highly reactive, oil-soluble or organometallic form of Aluminum is required such as unique oil-soluble Aluminum compound (20% Aluminum) for use in fuel additives. Two aspects of Aluminum's effect on Sodium are recognized.

  1. Form a very high melting Sodium Meta Aluminate (NaAlO2, m.p.of 1800ºC/ 3272ºF);

  2. React with some of the Magnesium to form a crystalline material known as spinel (MgAl2O4) which help to retain the combustion ash in the form of fine particles and in friable condition and this chemical effect eliminates the extremely sticky and low-melting deposits from mixtures of  Sodium and Vanadium oxides.

Failure of Aluminum oxide to provide a larger melting point increase (as would be expected from Table 3) is understood to be due not to the limitations of  Aluminum itself as a slag modifier but to the unreactivity of granular (Al2O3).

Silicon

Silicon is of special value for physical modification of boiler combustion ash-to keep the ash dry and friable. Oil soluble (organometallic) silicon components of additives, when burned with the fuel, form superfine particles of silicon dioxide which act to "coat" and dilute the particles. This effect of silicon at temperatures which include both the boiler and gas turbine operating regimes.

SO3 Generation and Cold-End Corrosion 

When sulfur containing fuels are burned in a boiler, the sulfur emerges largely as SO2; however, a very small amount of sulfur trioxide is also formed in the flame reaction. If  sulfur dioxide in the exhaust contact with Fe2O3 or V2O5 slag surfaces at temperatures of 500-6500C (900-12000F), and extra oxygen is present in the flue gases to react with it, SO3 will be formed in large quantities. This catalytic reaction is shown as follows:

SO2  + O2  (Fe2O3, V2O5) →  SO3

At temperatures below the acid dew point (generally less than 3000F, the sulfur trioxide condenses out with water vapor to form highly corrosive H2SO4. Condensation of this acid  on metal parts as in an air preheater or in a cold stack, will result in eventual failure of the corroded components from what is called cold-end corrosion. Reduction of sulfur trioxide is an important objective for boiler operator. There are three methods to solve that problem.

  1. Reduce excess air (and O2) to a point where SO2 to SO3 reaction can't occur.

  2. Endeavour to neutralize the acid as it condenses in the cold sections of the boiler.

  3. Prevent the acid from forming by eliminating the catalytic reaction in the high temperature regions of the boiler.

Suppress SO3

The most practical method of suppressing SO3 generation in boiler operation is to provide a continuing supply of superfine MgO having high specific surface area and reactivity to coat catalytic surfaces of iron oxide and V2O5. The most effective form of  MgO is that generated by the combustion of fuel containing an oil soluble Magnesium additive. At relatively low concentrations in relation to sulfur content of fuels, oil soluble Magnesium additives generating a nascent, superfine form of  MgO, provide a degree of effectiveness in suppressing the SO2-SO3 conversion reaction.

These applications involves fuels containing 5-25 ppm vanadium and 0.3-1.0% sulfur, satisfactory control (down to a few ppm SO3) is achieved by use of 3-5 gallons per thousand barrels, representing a modest cost of price such fuels (light blended residual oils). We employ severn science SO3 monitors to provide a continuous numerical record of SO3 control in fuel tests and applications.

When heavier boiler fuels are involved, ranging higher in either sulfur or vanadium content, we anticipate successful control at higher dosages. When both sulfur and vanadium contents are high, our experience suggests that SO3 control by even the best fuel treatment that may not be effective, so that applications involving this class of fuel must be undertaken with due caution

Smoking and Soot Emissions

Another problem in a boiler is that of smoking and inefficient combustion. This will always occur when the excess oxygen level is insufficient to combust the fuel completely, but it may also happen when a mechanical problem exists in the boiler, such as worn or improperly adjusted oil guns in the burners. If the soot and smoke generated by these problems is excessive, the fuel isn't being efficiently burned and the overall efficiency of the boiler suffers. Air pollution regulations may also be broken if the smoke is sufficient to exceed the local air quality specifications.

In correcting smoking problem, any needed mechanical adjustments should obviously be made first such as proper adjustment, cleaning of burner nozzles, etc. If smoking still occurs at reasonable excess air levels, an additive may be needed. here, an oil-soluble form of Mn or other metal is used to promote oxidation of the fuel. Through catalytic action of the Mn in the flame, the carbonaceous components of the fuel are more completely burned and soot and smoke are reduced. This obviously increases the efficiency of the boiler because more of the fuel's heat content goes toward producing steam, not smoke.

Smoke Control

Using [25% Mn (conc.), or its diluted version, and 10% Mn (dil.)] to provide a manganese level of about 25 to 50 ppm, may result in a dramatic lossening of visible smoke. This may be sufficient to allow substantial reductions in excess air to be made. In principle, major reductions in excess oxygen can result in substantial improvements in overall boiler efficiency (in some cases, as high as 5%).

Barium, while it may be less effective as a soot scavenger, seems also to extra prior, in flame action to prevent soot particle formation. Because of this difference in their mechanism, barium may be more efficient than Mn combustion improves in specific applications. Thus, use of a composition [25% Ba (conc.), or its diluted form and 10% Ba (dil.)] to provide  a Barium level of 25-100 ppm may be effective if insufficient manganese response is observed.

Thus, effective additive requirements for smoke suppression in boiler can't be predicted simply from fuel composition and designation of equipment and operating parameters. In applying smoke suppression additives, there is substantial need for a convenient field instrument like the Von Brand Meter for monitoring gas turbine, boiler, and diesel smoke suppression.

 

 

 

 

 

Recalculation of ppm-Results to mg/m3

Basics: Avogardro's Number, Ideal Gas Law

ppm = parts per million  (by mass or by volume:   mg/kg or ml/m3 not: mg/l)

A similar unit is % = parts per hundred           1% = 10.000 ppm

The volume share is relevant when talking about gases !

 

6.023* 1023 atoms or molecules   = 1 mole       (Avogardro's Number)

1 mole of each gas                        = 22.41  liters  (Ideal Gas Law)

The mass of 1 mole                       = Molecular weight in grams

 

e.g.  Carbon monoxide:         C   O

                                              ↓    ↓

        Atomic weights:            12+16 = 28      Molecular weight

 

Therefore:  1 mole of CO has a weight of 28 g and a volume of 22.41 l.

 

Recalculation procedure:

    mg/m3 =  ppm * molecular mass / 22.41

 

e.g. CO:  mg/m3 =  ppm * 28 / 22.41
