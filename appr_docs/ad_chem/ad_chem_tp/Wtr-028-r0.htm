Standard Test Method for Nitrite Ion  in Water

Introduction

This method covers the determination of nitrite ion  in water  by using volumetric method.

The first draft was initiated by chemist : Amal El-Gayar; reviewed by department director chemist: Zakia Soltan; revised by quality control chemist: Mohamed Mosbah Mokpel and accepted by Eng. Awad M. Fatthy; Head Sector of Talkha Power Station

Significance

This method covers the titrimetric determination of nitrite in water and is applicable to waters containing nitrite ion in concentrations from about 3–600 mg/L (ppm). Higher concentration may be determined by employing more concentrated potassium permanganate and sodium oxalate solutions.

The method is applicable where a complete freedom from interferences isn't required and is particularly applicable in systems containing a nitrite content greater than 5 ppm.

Summary of  Method

A measured volume of sample is added below the surface of unknown volume of acidified potassium  permanganate KMnO4.Under these conditions nitrite ions react only with permanganate ions. The solution is heated to complete the reaction. Excess permanganate is determined by adding a measured amount of sodium  oxalate (excess), followed by back titration with standard permanganate solution.

Interferences

Reducing agents interfere with this method since it is essentially a means of measuring the reducing tendency. This does not detract from the utility of the method in many cases; for example, when nitrite is added to industrial water as a corrosion inhibitor and is present in relatively large quantities so that a small error is of little consequence. However, in those instances where relatively large quantities of other reducing agents are present the method is of lesser application.

Sampling

Nitrite ion should be determined as soon as possible after sampling, even when sterile bottles are used  since bacteria naturally present in  the water may cause conversion of all  or part of nitrite to other materials such as nitrate  or ammonia.

Keeping the sample  cold, below 4.4oC ( 40oF ), is helpful in minimizing nitrite conversion.  

Safety Precautions

  • Collect the sample in sterile bottles.

  • The reagent used must be of sufficiently high purity  to permit its use without lessening the accuracy of determination.

  • Water used for this method shall be free of nitrite ion and preferably sterile. This sterile water can be prepared by heating in an autoclave.

  • Nitrite-free water can be obtained by distillation, but preferably by the demineralization of distilled water, taking care to prevent absorption of nitrous fumes in the prepared water.

  • The pipette must be yield the required volume in one filling.

Tools and Equipment

 

Apparatus

Pipettes Since, this method depends on the amount of KMnO4 consumed, it is imperative that pipetts for delivering the potassium permanganate standard solution and the sodium oxalate standard solution be calibrated and cleaned for maximum precision.

Reagents

 

Potassium Permanganate Standard Solution (0.04 N).

  • Dissolve 2.5 gm of potassium permanganate in 2 liters of boiling water in a Florence  flask.

  • Cover with a beaker and simmer for ½ h.

  • Cool to room temperature.

  • Filter twice by gravity through a fritted-glass filter and store in a dark-colored–stoppered  glass  bottle.

  • Pipette triplicate 50 ml aliquots of the Na2C2O4 solution (0.04 N) into a 500-ml Erlenmeyer flask and add 100 ml of water

  • Heat to 90°C and add 10 ml of sulfuric acid H2SO4 (1+1).

  • Titrate rapidly with  potassium permanganate to a slight pink end point, persistent for 1 min.

  • Calculate the normality of the KMnO4 solution. If necessary, adjust the normality to slightly less than the normality of the  Na2C2O4 solution and restandardize.

Sodium Oxalate Standard Solution (0.04 N)

Dry national bureau (Na2C2O4) oxidimetric grade sodium oxalate, or equivalent, at 105°C for 1h. and cool in a desiccator. Weigh 5.360 g, dissolve in water, and dilute to 2.0 liters. Store in a chemical resistant borosilicate glass container. Sodium oxalate solutions are subject to deterioration by microorganisms and should be prepared at time of use.

Sulfuric acid (1 + 1)  Carefully mix 1 volume of conc. H2SO4 ( Sp.Gr.1.84 ) into 1 volume of  water.

Work to be Carried out

  1. Add 50 ml of KMnO4 solution and 10 ml of H2SO4 (1+1 ) for each 50 ml sample up to a max. of 30 ml of acid to a 500 ml  Erlenmeyer flask.

  2. Pipette a volume of sample containing 0.6 to 30.0 mg of nitrite ion to the flask by immersing the tip of the pipette below the surface of the KMnO4 solution.

  3. A suitable sample volume is shown in Table 1.

  4. Warm the solution in the flask to 90°C immediately, the color change from purple color to brownish.

  5. Add 50 ml of Na2C2O4 solution and swirl until colorless.

  6. Titrate with KMnO4 solution to a faint pink end point.

  7. Determine the blank at the time of analysis, by repeating the procedure at least twice, using water equal in volume to the sample.

N.B.  Nitrite solutions, especially in the presence of microbiological contamination, are unstable, so must be analyzed the test with minimum delay.

Calculation 

 Calculate concentration of nitrite ion in the sample in mg/L

 

       NO2 , mg/L (ppm) = { ( VX – Va )  x N x 23 004 } / Vs

 

         Where:

      Vx =  ml of KMnO4 solution required for titration of the sample,

      Va =  ml of KMnO4 solution required for titration of the blank,

      N  =  normality of KMnO4 solution and

      Vs =  ml of sample used.

Precision

The precision of this method may be expressed as follow :

 

       SLO = ( 0.00204 X + 0.16 )

 

Where

SLO = Single–laboratory–single–operator precision in milligrams per liter of  NO2

X = Concentration of nitrite determined in milligrams per liter of NO2.
