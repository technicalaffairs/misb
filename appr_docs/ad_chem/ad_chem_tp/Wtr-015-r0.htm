Introduction

This document outlines the standard test method for hydrazine in water. The method covers the colorimetric determination of hydrazine in water.

Significance

Hydrazine is a man-made chemical and is not found in natural waters. The determination of hydrazine is usually made on boiler feedwaters, process waters and other waters that have been treated with hydrazine ( N2H4 ) to prevent corrosion by dissolved oxygen.

This reducing chemical reacts with dissolved oxygen to form nitrogen and water. However, under certain conditions it can also decompose to form ammonia and nitrogen. Hydrazine is used extensively as a preboiler treatment chemical for high-pressure boiler to scavenge small amounts of dissolved oxygen, that are not removed by mechanical deaeration. It has the advantage over sulfite treatment in that it does not produce any dissolved solids in the boiler water. Hydrazine is often determined in concentrations below 0.1 mg / liter. However, in layup solutions for the protection of idle boilers, hydrazine may be present in concentrations as high as 200 mg / liter.

Additionally, hydrazine provides protection to carbon steel by chemically reducing the unprotective layers of ferric oxide ( Fe2O3 ) to a more adherent protective layer of magnetic ( Fe3O4 ).

Summary of Method

Para-dimethylaminobenzaldhyde produces a specific, yellow reaction product with hydrazine. The intensity of the yellow color is proportional to the amount of hydrazine in the water, and follows Beer's law.

Interferences

The substances  normally present in industrial water don’t interfere with the test; however, the hydrazine content may be diminished by oxidizing agents collected with the sample or absorbed by it prior to testing.

Colors in the prescribed wavelengths also interfere, as do other dark colors or turbidities that cannot be overcome by the prescribed treatment.

Tools and Equipment

Apparatus

Colorimeter-  It is suitable for measurements at 425 nm or 458 nm. Certain photoelectric filter photometers are capable of measurements at 425 nm, but not at 458 nm. Measurements may be made at 425 nm, with a reduction in sensitivity of approximately 50 % of that possible at 458 nm.

Reagents

Purity of Reagents- All reagents shall conform to the specification of the Committee on Analytical Reagents of the American Chemical Society, where such specification is available. Other grades may be used that the reagent is of sufficiently high purity to permit its use without lessening the accuracy of the determination.

Hydrazine, Standard Solution (1ml = 0.1 N2H4)– Dissolve 0.328 g of hydrazine dihydrochloride ( N2H4 . 2HCl ) in 100 ml of water and 10 ml of HCl ( sp gr 1.19 ). Dilute with water to 1 liter in a volumetric flask, and mix.

Hydrochloric Acid (sp gr 1.19 )– Concentrated hydrochloric acid ( HCl ).

Hydrochloric Acid ( 1 + 9 )- Mix 1 volume of HCl (sp gr 1.19) with 9 volume of water.

Hydrochloric Acid ( 1 + 99 )- Mix 1 volume of HCl (sp gr 1.19) with 99 volume of water.

Para-dimethylaminobenzaldehyde Solution- Dissolve 4.0 gm of para-dimethylaminobenzaldehyde in 200 ml of methyl alcohol ( CH3OH) and 20 ml of HCl (sp.gr.1.19). Store in dark bottle out of direct sunlight.

Purity of Water- Water shall be freshly boiled and free of dissolved oxygen.

Sampling

Analyze the sample as soon after collection as practicable, since hydrazine undergoes auto-oxidation as well as oxidation by oxidizing agents. Such agents may be in the sample or may enter the sample from the atmosphere.

If it is suspected that oxidation of the hydrazine in the sample is occurring in the interval between collection and analysis, or if the sample is not to be analyzed immediately, collect under acid. Place 5.0 ml of HCl (1+9) in a 50-ml volumetric flask, and collect sufficient sample in the flask to bring the total volume to 50 ml.

Work to be Carried Out

Calibration

Prepare a series of hydrazine standards by making appropriate dilutions of the hydrazine solution (1ml = 0.1mg N2H4 ) with HCl ( 1+99 ), so that a 50 ml aliquot of each dilution will contain the desired quantity of hydrazine( 0.2 to 5.0 µ g ) .

Pipette 50 ml portions of hydrazine standard solutions into 100 ml beakers, flasks, or cylinders and proceed as directed in the following procedure, except do not add more acid. Plot transmittance against micrograms of hydrazine.

A separate calibration curve must be made for each photometer and a recalibration must be made if it is necessary to change the cell, lamp, or filter, or if any alterations of instrument or reagents are made.Check the curve for each series of tests by running two or more solutions of known hydrazine concentrations.

Procedure

  1. Add from a burette exactly 5.0 ml of HCl (1+9) to a 100 ml beaker, flask, or cylinder, unless the sample was collected under acid, in which case, proceed directly as described in the next step, except to use HCl (1+99) instead of water for diluting to 50 ml.

  2. Transfer to the 100 ml beaker, flask or cylinder a portion of the sample that will contain approximately 0.20 to 5.0 µg of N2H4 by means of a graduated pipette. Add water from a graduated burette or pipette to make a final volume of exactly 50 ml, and mix. Add 10 ml of p-dimethylaminobenzaldehyde solution with a pipette, and mix. Let the mixture stand at least 10 min and not more than 100 min. Measure and compare the color by means of the colorimeter. Make Photometric measurements at approximately 458 nm.

  3. Prepare a blank containing no added hydrazine in order to correct for color of the unreacted p-dimethylaminobenzaldehyde and any optical effects involved in adjusting the photometer for100 % transmission, in photometric procedures, with water showing no appreciable color.

  4. Prepare a blank by using the sample, after having first oxidized the hydrazine in the sample by suitable means, in the case of highly–colored water.Determine the completeness of the oxidation of the hydrazine by making a plot of the percentage transmittance ( compared to water to which the reagents have been added ) versus wavelength for the spectral region 400 to500 nm, and nothing whether the characteristic dip of p–dimethylaminobenzalazine occurs at 458 nm.

(Note) Measurement may be made at 425 nm reduce sensitivity about 50 % of that possible at 458 nm.

Calculation

Calculate the hydrazine concentration in milligrams per liter as follow:

            Hydrazine, (mg/liter) =  W / S

Where:

W = micrograms of hydrazine found in accordance with the procedure.

S =  milliliters of samples.

Precision

The precision may be expressed as follows:

         So = ( 0.99 X + 0.041 ) / S

         St = ( 1.08 X + 0.081 ) / S

Where:

So = single-operator precision expressed in milligrams per liter of hydrazine,

St = overall precision expressed in milligrams per liter of hydrazine,

X = concentration of hydrazine determined in milligrams per liter, and

S = milliliters of sample taken for test.
