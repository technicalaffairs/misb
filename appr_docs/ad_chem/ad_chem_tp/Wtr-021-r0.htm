Introduction

This document outlines the testing procedure P1, which must be performed on a weekly basis as indicated by W1 in the above title block

Copper salts are used in water supply systems to control biological growths in reservoirs and distribution pipes and to catalyze the oxidation of manganese. Corrosion of copper containing alloys in pipe fittings may introduce measurable amount of copper into the water in a pipe system.

This document outlines the experiment used in copper concentration determination. The experiment is known as copper neocoproine method.

The first draft initiated by chem. Mohamed A. Farrag, tested by chem. Esam Abdel Aziz Mohamed, reviewed by director chem. Mohamed Kamal, revised by EDEPCo. quality control chemist Wael A. Tantawy, and approved by eng. Ahmed El-Refaey; Head Sector of Abu Sultan Power Station.

Significance of Method

Cuprous ion in neutral or slightly acidic solutions reacts with neocoproine (2,9 dimethyl 1,10 phenanthroline) to form a yellow colored complex. The complex can be extracted by a number of organic solvents including a chloroform-methanol mixture. The reaction is virtually specific for copper; the colour follows Beer's law up to a concentration of 0.2 mg Cu/25 ml solvent. The full colour development is obtained when the pH of the aqueous solution is between 3 and 9. The color is stable in CHCl3-CH3OH for several days.

Sampling and Storage

Copper ion tends to be adsorbed on the surface of sample containers. Therefore, analyze samples as soon as possible after collection. If storage is necessary, use 0.5 mL 1+ 1 HCl / 100 mL sample to prevent this adsorption.

Minimum Detectable Concentration

The minimum detectable concentration to 0.01 absorbance is 3 µg Cu when a 1 cm cell is used and 0.6 µg Cu when a 5 cm cell is used.

 

Safety Precautions

  • The sample must be taken as the applicable ASTM methods D 3370-76.

  • Acids such as hydrochloric acid and others are dangerous, so you have to wear breathing mask.

  • Keep these acids closed. Don't get it in the eyes, on the skin, or on clothing. Don't breathe them, use only in a hood.

  • Store ammonium hydroxide solution in polyethylene bottle.

Tools and Equipment

Apparatus

  • Spectrophotometer for use at 457 nm, providing a light path of 1 cm or longer.

  • Separatory funnels 125 ml with glass or TFE stopcock and stopper.

  • Volumetric flasks 100 ml.

  • Hot plate.

 

 

 

Reagents:

Redistilled Water: Copper free, distilled water passed through an ion exchange unit.

Hydroxylamine Hydrochloride: Dissolve 50 gm NH2OH.HCl in 450 ml water. The sample is treated with it to reduce cupric ions to cuprous ions.                         

Ammonium Hydroxide (5 N): Dilute 330 ml conc. NH4OH (28-29%) to 1000 ml with water store in a polyethylene bottle.

Congo Red Paper: Or other pH test paper (showing a color change in the pH range of 4-6).

Neocoproine Reagent: Dissolve 100 mg of neocoproine (2,9 dimethyl 1,10 phenanthroline hemihydrate) in 100 ml methanol.

Stock Copper Solution: In a 250 ml conical flask, add 200 mg copper wire or foil, 10 ml of water and 5 ml conc nitric acid, warm gently to complete dissolution of copper and boil to expel oxides of nitrogen. Cool and complete to 1000 ml with water using volumetric flask (1ml = 200 µg Cu).

Standard Copper Solution: Dilute 50 ml stock copper solution to 500 ml with water (1ml = 20 µg Cu).

Sodium Citrate Solution: Dissolve 150 gm sodium citrate Na3C6H5O7.2H2O in 400 ml water. Add 5 ml hydroxyl amine hydrochloride solution and 10 ml neocoproine reagent. Extract with 50 ml CHCl3 to remove copper impurities and discard CHCl3 layer. It is used to complex metallic ions that might precipitate when the pH is raised.

Work to be Carried Out 

Preparation of calibration curve

  1. Pipette 50 ml water into a 125 ml separatory funnel for use as a reagent blank.

  2. Prepare standards by pipetting 1.00 to 10.00 ml (20 to 200 µg Cu) standard copper solution into a series of 125 ml separatory funnel, and dilute to 50 ml with water. Add 1 ml conc. H2SO4 and use the extraction procedure (see II ).

  3. Construct a calibration curve by plotting absorbance vs. µg of Cu.

To prepare a calibration curve for smaller amounts of copper:

  1. Dilute 10.0 mL standard copper solution to 100 mL. Carry 1.00 to 10.00 mL volumes of this diluted  standard through the extraction procedure (see II ), but use 5-cm cells to measure absorbance.

Treatment of sample 

  1.  Transfer 100 ml sample to a 250 ml beaker, add 1ml conc. H2SO4 and 5 ml conc. HNO3.Add a few boiling chips and cautiously evaporate to dense white SO3 fumes on hot plate .If solution remains colored, cool, add another 5 ml conc. HNO3 and again repeat if necessary, until so becomes colorless.

  2. Cool, add 80 ml  H2O and bring to a boil, cool and filter into 100 ml volumetric flask and complete to 100 ml with water.

  3. Pipette 50 ml or other suitable portion contains 4 to 200 µg into a 125 ml separatory funnel, add 5 ml Hydroxylamine hydrochloride solution and 10 ml sodium citrate solution, and mix thoroughly.

  4. Adjust pH to 4 by adding NH4OH until Congo red paper is just definitely red.

  5. Add 10 ml neocoproine reagent and 10 ml chloroform. Stopper and shake vigorously for 30 sec. to extract the copper neocoproine complex into the chloroform.

  6. Let mixture separate into two layers and withdraw lower layer into a 25 ml volumetric flask taking care not to transfer any of the aqueous layer and dilute to 25 ml with methanol, stopper ,mix

  7. Transfer an appropriate portion of extract to a suitable absorption cell (1cm for 40 to 200 µg Cu; 5 cm for lesser amounts).

  8. Measure absorbance at 457 nm or with 450-460 nm.

  9. Determine micrograms copper in final solution by reference to the appropriate calibration curve.

 Calculation

mg Cu/L  =  µg Cu (in 25 mL final volume)
