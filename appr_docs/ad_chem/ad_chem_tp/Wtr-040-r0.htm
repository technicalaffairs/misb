Standard Test Method for Phosphorus in Water.

Procedure

Using ASTM D 515
 Method B–Colorimetric Amino Reduction Method.

Approved Date: 07 March 2005

Introduction

This method is applicable to the routine determination of various form of phosphorus in waters containing from 0.1 to 3 mg/L (ppm) of phosphorus. When modified by the addition of bismuth salt to the acid reagent, it is applicable to waters containing 0.03 to 1 mg/L of phosphorus. The ranges, which can be extended upward by diluting a smaller aliquot to the prescribed volume, are for photometric measurements in a 20 mm cell.

Significance and Uses

Phosphorus is widely distributed in the environment as both inorganic and organically bound phosphates. Sources of phosphates include surface-applied fertilizers, cleaning and laundering products, boiler water conditioners, and drinking water treatment aids. Because phosphorus is a nutrient for photosynthetic organisms it may be important to monitor and control discharge into the environment.

Summary

Orthophosphate reacts with ammonium molybdate in an acid medium to form a molybdophosphate that in turn is reduced to a molybdenum blue complex with amino-naphthol-sulfonic acid. The color is proportional in intensity to the phosphorus concentration. When Bismuth salt is added to the sulfuric acid reagent, the intensity of the blue color developed in the test is increased about fourfold. Only orthophosphate forms a blue color in the test. Other forms of phosphorus such as polyphosphate and organic phosphorus compounds can be converted to orthophosphate by the various sample digestive  treatments prescribed in the method.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Sampling

The sample must be taken as the applicable ASTM methods D 3370 – 76.

When determining only dissolved phosphorus, filter the sample through a 0.45-m membrane filter after sampling.

Analysis is best performed immediately after collecting the sample to minimize possible change.

Preserve the sample by adding 40 mg of mercuric chloride per litre of sample and refrigerating it at 4oC if analysis can't be performed at the same day of collecting, and to prevent precipitation of the mercury, previously add sufficient sodium chloride to the sample, if necessary, so that chloride content will be at least 50 mg/L in the sample.

If total recoverable phosphorus only is to be determined preservation isn't needed.

Sample storage in glass bottles is suggested.

 

Interferences

  • Up to 40 mg/l (ppm) of iron, 75mg/L of chromate and 50,000 mg/l of chloride will not interfere with the test.

  • If more then than 75mg/L of chromate is present in the sample, the reference solution for the photometric measurement should not be water instead  It must be the sample treated with all reagents except the molybdate solution.

  • No more than 1500 mg/L of chloride should be present in the sample when the bismuth modification is followed.

  • Interference from nitrite can be overcome by adding 0.1g of sulfamic acid to the sample before adding the molybdate.

  • A silica concentration 20 times larger than that of the phosphorus will cause an error of less than 2% .

  • When sulfide is present in concentrations higher than several milligrams per liter, a blue color forms after adding the Molybdate. This interference can be eliminated by adding sufficient saturated potassium permanganate solution to the sample to produce a permanent weak pink color before adding the molybdate.

  • To prevent formation of calcium sulfate precipitate, 7 ml of concentrated hydrochloric acid should be used in place of sulfuric acid when calcium in the sample is greater than 200 mg/L.

  • Rapidity of the blue color development depends on temperature. It should be between 21 and 35oC ( 70 and 90oF ).

Safety Precautions

  • Make sure that the sample bottle is correctly labeled.

  • Make sure that the sample bottle is free from dust and dirt.

  • The sample must be kept away from exposure to direct sunlight.

  • Cleaning solvents such as acid dichromate, nitric acid and others are dangerous, so you have to wear breathing mask.

  • Keep this solvents closed. Do not get it in the eyes, on the skin, or on clothing. Do not breath them, use only in a hood.

 

Tools and Equipment

Apparatus

  • Photometer at 650 nm. ( applied to 20–ml cell )

  • Acid–Washed Glassware; All glassware and sampling bottles shall be soaked in hydrochloric acid (1+ 3) to remove possible phosphate coating and rinsed with water prior to use.

Reagent

  1. Amino Solution– Dissolve in 100 ml of water the following chemicals in given order; 3.7 g of sodium sulfite(Na2SO3), 0.1g of 1-amino 2-naphthol 4–sulfonic acid, and 6.2 g of sodium metabisulfite ( sodium pyrosulfite, Na2S2O5 ). Store the solution in an amber bottle and make it up fresh every 2 weeks.

  2. Ammonium Molybdate Solution (48g/L)– Dissolve 48g of ammonium molybdate
    [(NH4)6Mo7O24 .4H2O] in about 800 ml of water. Add 2.5 ml of ammonium hydroxide (NH4OH, sp gr 0.90) and dilute it to 1 L with water.

  3. Ammonium Persulfate- Crystalline ammonium persulfate ((NH4)2S2O8). Potassium persulfate ( K2S2O8) is a suitable replacement for the ammonium salt.

  4. Phosphorus Standard Solution (1ml = 0.025mg of phosphorus )–  First prepare a stock solution (1 ml = 0.05 mg of phosphorus) by dissolving in water 0.2197 g of potassium dihydrogen phosphate (KH2PO4) that has been dried for 1 h in an oven at 105oC, and diluting the solution to exactly 1 L with water. Prepare the standard solution by diluting 50.0 ml of the stock solution to 100 ml with water in a volumetric flask.

  5. Sulfuric Acid (37+63)– Add 370 ml of concentrated sulfuric acid (H2SO4,sp gr 1.842) slowly to about 600 ml of water. Cool and dilute it to 1L with water.

  6. Sulfuric Acid ( 37+63, Bismuth added )- Dissolve 1.200 g of bismuth nitrate (Bi(NO3)3.5H2O) in 250 ml of H2SO4 (37+63) while still warm.

Work to be Carried out

 

Calibration

For a range from 0.1 to 3 mg/L (ppm)

  1. Prepare a series of standard phosphate solutions to cover the range from 0 to 3 mg / L. Make up the standards by diluting suitable volumes of phosphorus standard solution (1 ml = 0.025 mg of phosphorus ) to 100 ml with water.

  2. Develop color in the series of standards as directed in the following procedure (part 1).

  3. Measure the absorbance of each standard at 650 nm in a 20-mm cell using water as the reference solution for the initial photometer setting to zero. Plot the absorbance value as the ordinate and the corresponding phosphorus concentrations as the abscissa on linear graph paper.

 

For a Range from 0.03 to 1 mg/L (ppm)

  1. Prepare a series of standard phosphate solutions to cover the range from 0 to 1.0 mg / L, make up the standards by diluting suitable volumes of phosphorus standard solution ( 1 ml = 0.025 mg of phosphorus ) to 100 ml with water.

  2. Develop color in the series of standards as directed in the following procedure (part 2). Measure the absorbance of each standard at 650 nm in a 20 – mm cell using water as the reference solution for the initial photometer setting to zero. Plot the absorbance value as the ordinate and the corresponding phosphorus concentrations as the abscissa on linear graph paper.

Procedure

Part 1

a) Orthophosphate, Range from 0.1 to 3 mg/L of phosphorus

  1. Transfer 100 ml of the clear sample containing 3mg /l of phosphorus or less into a 250 ml Erlenmeyer flask. For higher concentrations use a smaller sample and dilute it to 100 ml with water.
    Note:         Filtering the sample through a 0.45 µm membrane filter will normally remove particulate matter for the determination of dissolved orthophosphate in boiler waters. However, even a retentative filter paper may not be satisfactory if the boiler contains a dispersive. If a filter paper is to be used, add 4 g of potassium nitrate (KNO3) and 2 to 4 ml of sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl) solution and boil it several minutes to flocculate the precipitated matter before filtering.

  2. Add the following reagents in the given order to the sample, mixing after each addition: 5 ml of H2SO4 (37+63), 5 ml of ammonium Molybdate solution, and 5 ml of amino solution, allow no less than 9 min, nor more than (11 min) for color development.

  3. Measure the absorbance of the test sample at 650 nm, employing water as the reference solution. When the chromate is higher than 75 mg/L in the sample analyzed use the method described in interference section before. Record the milligrams per litre of phosphorus indicated by the calibration curve established before.

(b) Hydrolysable Phosphorus + Orthophosphate, Range from 0.1 to 3 mg/L of Phosphorus

  1. Proceed in accordance with part 1a.

  2. Add 5ml of H2SO4,(37+63) to the sample, use 7 ml of concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl, sp gr 1.19) instead of 5 ml of H2SO4 (37+63) if the calcium in the sample is more than 200 mg/l. Boil the solution for at least 30 min on a hot plate, adding water to keep the volume between 25 to 100 ml, alternatively, heat the solution for 10 min in an autoclave or pressure cooker at 103 to 138 kPa (15 to 20 psi).  Cool the solution to about 25oC (77oF). Do not over chill. Dilute the solution to 105 ml with water by means of a plastic graduated cylinder having a mark at the 105-ml level.

  3. Add 5 ml of ammonium Molybdate solution and 5 ml of amino solution, mixing after each addition. Allow no less than 9 min, nor more than 11 min for color development.

  4. Measure the absorbance of the test sample at 650 nm to obtain hydrolysable phosphorus + orthophosphate.

(c) Total Phosphorus, Range from 0.1 to 3 mg/L of Phosphorus

  1. Analyze the sample in accordance with (b), but add 1.0 g of ammonium persulfate to the sample after step (1).

Part 2

Phosphorus, Range from 0.03 to 1 mg/L of Phosphorus

  1. Analyze the sample in accordance with (a), (b) and (c) to determine the various forms of phosphorus, but in use H2SO4 (37+63, bismuth added) in place of  H2SO4 (37+63). Record the milligrams per litre of phosphorus indicated by the calibration curve established before.

Calculation 

Calculate the concentration of the various form of phosphorus in milligrams per liter

 

( parts per million ) of phosphorus by applying the following equation for orthophosphate determined in part 1 and part 2.

Orthophosphate, mg/l of phosphorus = A × (100/B)

  Where:

            A = milligrams per liter of phosphorus indicated by the calibration curve.

                      B = milliliters of sample analyzed.

 

Hydrolyzable Phosphorus = ( Hydrolyzable Phosphorus + Orthophosphate Phosphorus)– (Orthophosphate Phosphorus)

Organic Phosphorus = (Total Recoverable Phosphate) – ( HydrolyzablePhosphorus + Orthophosphate Phosphorus )

Particulate Phosphorus = (Total Recoverable Phosphorus) – (Total Dissolved Phosphorus)

Precision

The precision of the method for dissolved orthophosphate:

In the range from 0.1 to 3 mg/L of phosphorus can be expressed as follows:

ST = 0.65 a / S,  and

SO = (0.52 a + 0.55) / S

In the range from 0.03 to 1 mg/L of phosphorus, the precision is:

ST =(1.4 a – 0.31) / S  and

SO = (0.31 a) / S

Where:

ST = over–all precision , mg/L

          SO = single–operator precision , mg/L

          a   =  concentration of phosphate determined, mg/L   and

          S  =  milliliters of sample used.
