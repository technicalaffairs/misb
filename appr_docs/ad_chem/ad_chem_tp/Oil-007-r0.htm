Introduction

This document outlines the testing procedures, which must be performed on an annually bases as indicated by (I1-Y1) in the above title block, and according to the Directives of the Central Chemical Laboratories.

(a) These methods are suitable for the determination of the acidity of used and unused lubricating oil, fuel oil, and petrolatum. Oil containing additives may give erroneous results.

(b) Methods A and B are alternatives for the determination of total acidity. Method A is more suitable than B for dark colored oils of viscosity higher than 120 cSt at 100οF and petrolatums. Method B is often preferred for pale colored, low viscosity oils such as transformer oil. Method C determines the organic acidity.

(c) If there is elemental sulphur in solution, method A should be used, as method B may give rise to erroneous results due to interference with the indicator.

Significance      

The neutralization value of an oil is a measure of the acidic constituents or contaminants in the oil. Its value, negligible in an unused oil, increases as a result of oxidative ageing and is used as a general guide for determining when an oil should be replaced or reclaimed, provided suitable rejection limits have been established and confirmation received from other tests.

Definitions

(a) The Total Acidity is a measure of the combined organic and inorganic acidity.

(b) The Inorganic Acidity is a measure of the mineral acid present.

(c) The Organic Acidity is obtained by deducting the inorganic acidity from the total acidity.

 

Outline of Method 

For total acidity the sample is dissolved in a solvent and the solution titrated with 0.1 N alkali. The inorganic acidity is determined by extracting the sample with warm water and titrating the extract with 0.1 N alkali.

 

 

Safety Precautions

  • The sample must be taken as MPIS - procedure no. TD-SS-003-r0.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is correctly labeled.   

  • Thoroughly clean and dry the accessories before starting the test.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is free from dust and dirt.

  • The sample must be kept away from exposure to direct sunlight.

  • As oil sample must be free from water it has to be taken under dry and clear environmental conditions.

  • Avoid oil spillage to the ground.

Tools and Equipment

  • Conical flask.

  • Measuring flask.

  • Heater.

  • Pipette: 1ml transfer pipette, graduated in 0.01 and 0.1 ml divisions, respectively.

Reagents

(a)  Standard Potassium Hydroxide Solution (alcoholic, 0.1N)

Purify 95% ethanol or IMS 66 op with silver oxide in the following manner:

-Dissolve 1.5 g silver nitrate in about 3 ml of water, add to 1 liter of alcohol in a glass-stoppered bottle, and   mix thoroughly.
-Dissolve 3 g of potassium hydroxide in 10-15 ml of warm alcohol. Cool and add slowly to the alcoholic silver nitrate solution with slight stirring and allow to settle for not less than1week.
-Filter the clear supernatant solution and distil off the alcohol on a steam bath.
-Dissolve 6 g solid potassium hydroxide in 1 liter of the purified alcohol and allow the solution to settle in a dark place.Decant, or filter the clear solution through glass wool. allow to stand for 24 hr and standardize.
-Restandardization of  the solution each day that it is used.

(b) Phenolphthalein Solution- 0.5% alcoholic.

(c) Methyl Orange Solution- 0.1% aqueous.

(d) Alkali Blue Solution- Extract 2 g of alkali blue 6B with boiling alcohol by means of a soxhlet apparatus, filter it if necessary, and dilute the solution to 100 ml with alcohol.

(e) Ethyl Alcohol- 95% by volume or IMS 66 op.

(f) Toluene- Conforming to the current IP specification.

Work to be Carried Out

Method A: Total Acidity

Procedure

  1. Weigh approximately 10 g of  sample to the nearest 0.01 g into a 250-ml conical flask; a larger sample may be used for oils of  low acidity.

  2. Add 1-3 ml of alkali blue solution and a drop of approx 0.1 N hydrochloric acid to a mixture of 60 ml of toluene and 40 ml of alcohol in another flask  to sensitize the indicator.

  3. Neutralize the mixture with the potassium hydroxide solution. In neutralizing the mixture do not at the preliminary purple-red, but obtain a strong wine-red color.Add the neutralized mixture to the sample, with swirling. If the mixture assumes a purple-blue color after the sample is entirely dissolved, titrate it immediately with the potassium hydroxide solution at a temperature not exceeding 30οC.

  4. Consider the end-point reached when the purple-blue color changes into a pronounced wine-red color which persists for 15 sec.or reverses with 2 drops of 0.1 N acid.Do not prolong the titration unnecessarily, as contamination with carbon dioxide appreciably affects the result. This effect can be obviated by the use of an atmosphere of nitrogen and this procedure is recommended for reference purposes.

Calculation and Reporting

(a) Calculate the total acidity of the sample from the formula:
                         AT  = 56.1 NV/ W
where AT  is the total acidity in mg KOH/g of sample;
          V            Volume of potassium hydroxide solution in ml;
          N            Normality of the potassium hydroxide solution;
         W            Weight of sample in g.

(b) Report the result to the nearest 0.05 mg KOH/g of sample as the Total Acidity, IP 1, Method A.

Method B: Total Acidity
Procedure

  1. Weigh approximately 10 g of sample to the nearest 0.01 g into a 250-ml conical flask; a larger sample may be used for oils of low acidity.

  2. Add 1 ml of the phenolphthalein solution to 50 ml of alcohol in another flask, heat the mixture to 40-50οC and neutralize it with the potassium hydroxide solution.

  3. Add  neutralized alcohol to sample and heat mixture to boiling on a water-bath or hot-plate.

  4. Allow it to boil for 5 min and agitate it by swirling to ensure extraction of the acids by the alcohol.

  5. Add a further 1 ml of phenolphthalein solution, cool the mixture to 40-50οC, and titrate it as quickly as possible with the potassium hydroxide solution.

Calculation and Reporting

(a) Calculate and report the total acidity as described in calculation of Method A.

(b) Report the result to the nearest 0.05 mg KOH/g of sample as the total acidity, IP1, Method B.

Method C: Organic  Acidity

Procedure

Determine the total acidity of the sample as described in Methods A or B and the inorganic acidity according to IP 182.

Calculation and Reporting

 (a)  Calculate the organic acidity of the sample by subtracting the inorganic acidity from the total acidity

 (b) Report the result to the nearest 0.05 mg KOH/g of sample as the Organic Acidity, IP 1, Method C.

NOTE 1- Observation of the end-point in dark-coloured solutions is made easier by the use of a narrow-bore glass tube as a stirrer. During the titration this is used to withdraw temporarily a few drops of the solution for observation by transmitted light.
