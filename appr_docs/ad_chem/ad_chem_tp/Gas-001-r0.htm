This method describes the calculation of calculating calorific value and specific gravity (relative density) of gaseous fuels from its components.

Natural gas is a vital component of the world's supply of energy. lt is one of the cleanest, safest, and most useful of all energy sources.

Natural gas burning is clean  and emits lower levels of potentially harmful byproducts into the air.

Summary of Method

The ideal gas calorific value and ideal gas specific gravity (relative density) are calculated from the molar composition and the respective ideal gas  values for the components. These values are then corrected to the real gas basis by means of a calculated compressibility factor.

 Significance and Use

The calorific value is a measure of the value of a pure gas or a gas mixture for use as a fuel. It indicates the maximum amount of heat that can be obtained by burning a unit of the gas, for the use of gases as heating agents. The relative merits of gases from different sources and having different compositions can be compared readily on the basis of their calorific values. The calorific value is, therefore, used as a parameter for determining the price of the gas. It is also an essential factor in calculating the efficiencies of energy conversion devices such as gas-fired turbines.

The specific gravity (relative density) of gas describes whether the gas is less or more dense than air. This determines whether the gas will rise rapidly or tend to collect near the ground before dispersing after a leak or spill. It is an important factor in calculations concerning flow through pipes and orifices, and is used in calculating relationships between amounts of gas measured volumetrically and the corresponding mass or molar amounts.         

 

 

 

Definitions

British Thermal Unit (Symbol Btu)

It's defined as the quantity of heat required to raise the temperature of one pound of water through one degree Fahrenheit (60 – 61oF).

                        1 kcal = 3.968 B.Th.U.

                or     1 B.Th.U = 252 cal = 0.252 kcal.

Compressibility Factor (Z )

The ratio of the actual volume of  a given quantity of gas at a specified temperature and pressure to its volume calculated from the ideal gas law at the same conditions.

Gross or High Calorific Value  of a Gas

The total amount of heat produced when one unit of the fuel has been burnt completely and the products of combustion have been cooled to room temperature of usually 60oF (15.6oC).

Lower or Net Calorific Value of a Gas 

The net heat produced when unit mass/volume of the fuel is burnet completely and the products are permit to escape. Alternatively net or lower calorific value:

= Gross C.V. - latent heat of water vapour formed

= Gross C.V. - Mass of hydrogen × 9 × latent heat of  steam

because 1 part by mass of hydrogen  produces 9 parts by mass of water. The latent heat of steam is 587 kcal/kg or 1060 B.Th.U./Ib of water vapour formed at room temperature i.e.15oC.

Specific Gravity (Relative Density) 

The ratio of the density of the gaseous fuel, under specific conditions of temperature and pressure to the density of dry air of normal carbon dioxide content, at the same temperature and pressure.

Calculation

  1. Calculate the ideal gas calorific value H  and ideal gas specific gravity (relative density), G  as follows.

H = x1H1+x2H2 + .....xnHn

G = x1G1+x2G2 + .....xnGn

where

x1 , x2 ,....xn  =  mole fractions of the component,

H1, H2 ....Hn  =  ideal gross calorific values and

G1, G2 …..Gn = ideal gas specific gravities (relative densities) of the components as given in Table 2 (dry basis).

  1. Calculate the compressibility factor Z , at 60 oF ( 15.6 oC) and 14.73 psia (101.56kpa) as follows: 

    Z     = 1-(x1b10.5 + x2b20.5 +….xnbn0.5)2 + (    2xH-xH2) × (0.0005)

where

 
b1 0.5, b2 0.5…..bn0.5 =  summation factors for the respective components other than hydrogen as given in Table 2.

 

xH = the mole fraction of hydrogen.

  1. Calculate the real gas calorific value Hr and real gas specific gravity (relative density ),Gr as follows:

             Hr (saturated ) = 0.9826H /Z

                 Hr (dry)=H /Z

                     Gr = 0.99959 G /Z

Note1: the vapor pressure of water at 60oF is 0.2561 psia. The Ratio of the volume of the dry gas to volume of the same quantity of saturated gas thus is (14.73-0.2561)/14.73 = 0.9826. The factor 0.99959 in the calculation of specific gravity is the compressibility of dry air at 14.73 psia and 60oF (15.6oC).

Note2: to convert calorific values to a cubic foot based on 60oF (15.6oC) and on standard atmosphere pressure (101.325 kpa), dry. Multiply Hr dry, by 0.99769.

Note3: to calculate net calorific value proceed as in calculations 1, 2 and 3 but in 1 use the ideal gas net calorific values of  Table 3 in place of the ideal gas total calorific values.
