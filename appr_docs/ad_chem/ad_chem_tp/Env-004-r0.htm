Introduction

Successful operation of the activated sludge process is dependent on laboratory control. Some of the laboratory procedures follow. These methods are presented as a group since they are interrelated. These groups are Settleability, Sludge Volume Index, Sludge Denisty Index, Sludge Age.

Sampling

 

Sampling, subsampling, and pipeting two-phase or three-phase samples may introduce serious errors. Make and keep such samples homogeneous during transfer.Use special handling to insure sample integrity when subsampling. Mix small samples with a magnetic stirrer.

Safety Precautions

  • Store sample in resistant-glass or plastic bottles.

  • Begin analysis as soon as possible because it's impractical to be preserved the sample.

  • If  necessary refrigerate sample at 4°C up to time of analysis to minimize microbiological decomposition of solids.

  • Bring samples to room temperature before analysis.

1- Settleability

 

Procedure

  1. Run the settleability test on activated sludge  immediately after the sample taken.

  2. Take the mixed liquor sample at the effluent end of the aeration tanks, while the return sludge sample should be taken at some points between the final settling tank and the point at which the sludge is mixed with the primary effluent.

  3. Determine the settleability of mixed liquor and return sludge by allowing 1000 ml of well-mixed samples of each to settle in 1000 ml graduated cylinders.

  4. Record the volume occupied by the sludge to the nearest 5 ml at the end of 30 min. Occasionally it is helpful to measure the sludge at various intervals of time, for example every 5,10, 20 and 30 min. The reading at the end of 30 min. generally is used for plant control.

  5. Although the settleability test on return sludge is not used in any of the calculation for activated sludge, the result is helpful in determining whather too much or too little sludge is being returned from the final settling tank.

2- Sludge Volume Index

The SVI (sludge volume index ) of activated sludge is defined as the volume in ml occupied by 1g of activated after settling for 30 min. From this definition it may be observed that the lower the SVI the better is the settling quality of the aerated mixed liquor. Also, high SVI values indicate poor settling qualities. Sludge with an SVI of 100 or less is considered a good settling sludge.

Calculation

The results obtained from the suspended matter test ( Water-033-r0 ) and settleability test on the aerated mixed liquor are used to obtain SVI.

 

                             ml of settled sludge in 30 min   ×  1,000

3- Sludge Denisty Index

The SDI (sludge density index) is also a method of measuring the settling quality of activated sludge. It differs from SVI (sludge volume index ) in that the higher the SDI value the better is the settling quality of aerated mixed liquor. Similarly, the lower the SDI, the poorer is the settling quality of the mixed liquor. SDI is the concentration in percent solids that the activated sludge with an SDI of one or more is considered to have good settling characteristics.

Calculation

The results obtained from the suspended matter test and settleability test on aerated mixed liquor also are used to obtain SDI.

4- Sludge Age

Sludge age also termed solids retention time and also mean cell residence time. It is defined as the ratio of the total weight of activated sludge in the plant to the weight of activated sludge lost from the system each day. This has proved to be a useful tool in controlling some plants. Generally the older the sludge, the higher will be the suspended matter and the lower will be the sludge volume index ( SVI ).

Calculation

                                                     ( v1C1 ) + ( V2C2 )

     Sludge Age, Days =  ---------------------------

                                       ( Q1 C3 ) + ( Q2C4 )

  where

V1 =   aeration tank volume, L

V2 =   settling tank volume, L

C1 =   aeration tank suspended matter concentration, mg/L

C2 =   settling tank suspended matter concentration, mg/L

C3 =   waste sludge suspended matter concentration, mg/L

C4 =   effluent wastewater suspended matter concentration, mg/L

Q1 =   waste sludge flow rate, L /day, and

Q2 =  effluent wastewater flow rate , L /day.
