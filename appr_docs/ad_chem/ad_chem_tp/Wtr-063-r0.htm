Introduction

Atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) is based on the absorption of light by atoms .All atoms can absorb light, but only at discrete wavelengths corresponding to the electronic configuration of the particular atom.

Flame AAS

Since AAS depends upon the fact that only free ground-state atoms absorb light at characteristic wavelengths, the efficiency of the atomization process is of primary importance. In flame AAS, the energy source for free atom production is heat; most commonly provided by introducing the sample into an air-acetylene or nitrous oxide-acetylene flame. For this atomizer it is crucial that the sample solution be converted into a fine spray or mist of tiny droplets before being introduced into the flame.

Graphite Furnace

The graphite furnace (GF) atomizer solves the two major problems of the flame atomizer:

  • Poor atomization efficiency and

  • Short residence times.

A graphite furnace is a tube that is connected to two low voltage electrodes. When a current is forced through the tube, the tube heats up. The amount of heating caused by the current flow can be accurately controlled, and the atomization of samples in a graphite furnace is usually performed over several heating steps.

AAS Measurement Involves

  1. Detecting the amount of  Cu in the closed cooling samples.
  2. Detecting the amount of  Mg & Ca  in the filter and clarifier samples.
  3. Detecting the amount of  V  in the light fuel oil samples.
  4. Detecting the amount of  Fe in the boiler water samples.

Safety Precautions

Personal Safety

  • Explosion and fire hazards exist which have the potential to cause death or serious injury to personal.

Electrical

  • The modules of the AI 1200 contain electrical circuits, components and devices operating at dangerous voltages. Contact with these circuits, components and/or devices can cause death, serious injury or electrical shock.

UV Radiation

  • Plasma excitation produces ultra-violet (UV) radiation, which can be harmful to human vision.  It is recommended that the operator wear UV safety glasses

  • Hollow cathode lamps (HCLs) also produce UV radiation but they do not pose a health hazard if the instrument is installed and operated in the prescribed manner.

Vapors and Fumes

  • The fume extractor/collection hood should be exhausted to the exterior of the building using a fan and ducting.

Compressed Gas Cylinders

Venting of any gas, except air, to the atmosphere (i.e. your laboratory) can cause death or serious  injury by asphyxiation, anesthetic effects, or poisoning.

  • Storage and use of compressed gas cylinders must be in strict compliance with local regulations.

Acetylene

Careless use of acetylene can cause explosion and/or fire, which may result in death, serious personal injury, burns and/or property damage.

  • Never use acetylene at pressures higher than 105 kPa (15 psi, 1.0 kg/cm2).

  • No part of the container should be subject to a temperature higher than 52°C.

  • Never bring acetylene into direct contact with copper, silver, mercury, gaseous chlorine, oil or grease. 

Burners

Improper or careless use of burners can cause explosion and/or fire, which may result  in death, serious personal injury, burns and/or property damage.

  • Whenever you handle burners, remember that the burner may be very hot.

  • Never use any fuel gas other than acetylene.

Nebulizers

  • Be sure that the nebulizer is firmly fitted to the spray chamber by the nebulizer fixing cover before lighting the flame. 

  • Never remove a nebulizer from the spray chamber while the flame is on.

Operation

 For Flame AAS

Ø Installing HC Lamps

  1. Take the cable whose socket is labeled #1 and attach it to the Copper HC lamp. Loosen the retaining screw on the turret position labeled #1 until the lamp will fit into the clamp

  2. Tighten the clamp until the lamp is held snugly.

  3. Click on the tab for the first lamp and enter ‘Cu’ in the ‘Element’ text box on the ‘Lamps’ tab of the Status window, Click the ‘Select’ button. This will rotate the turret until position #1 is facing the entrance window. When the lamp has been selected, the ‘Select’ button will be replaced by the lamp positioning controls and a light bulb will appear in the tab for lamp #1, indicating that it is the selected lamp.

Ø Opening a Method

  1. Select the ‘Open: Method’ option from the file menu. Select the Method named ‘Cu.’ from the file open dialog box. The Cu. method will be opened in a Method window. The Method window is very similar to the instrument status window. A Method contains all the parameters used to control the instrument and the software.

  2. Click on the ‘Copy Method to Instrument’ button located in the toolbar of the Method window to transfer the Method parameters to the Instrument. This transfers all instrument parameters to the "AI 1200" and starts setting up the hardware.

 The Main Window

Ø Optimizing the instrument

At this point, you have just installed the Cu lamp. Now, you must adjust the set up to optimize the signal.

  1. Go to the ‘Lamps’ tab of the Instrument Status window. At the bottom left of the window is the ‘Background Correction’ box. In the middle of the window is the ‘Entrance Slit’ section, where there is a text box showing the width of the entrance and exit slits in the monochromator. There is a pull-down menu for this tex tbox from which slit width can be selected. Be sure that the slit width is 0.2 nm.

  2. Adjust the monochromator’s diffraction grating for the wavelength of the lamp is the first step in optimizing the instrument is to. 

  3. Go to the ‘Spectrometer’ tab of the Instrument Status window.  In the ‘Monochromator’ section, change the wavelength in the textbox to that of the lamp selected

  4. Turn the lamp on. Go to the ‘Lamps’ tab of the Instrument Status window and enter “10” into the Peak Current textbox.  For most HC lamps, 10 mA is usually a good current to use when optimizing the instrument.

   Instrument Status – Spectrometer

  1. Go to the ‘Spectrometer’ tab of the Instrument Status window.  Be sure that the PMT voltage is set between 300 and 400 volts.  In the ‘Monochromator’ section, click on the ‘Peak WL’ button. This causes the monochromator to scan the wavelengths slightly longer and slightly shorter (approx. ±1 nm) than the one entered in the text box in order to find the highest intensity wavelength detected by the PMT. 

  2. Adjust the PMT voltage is the next step in optimizing the instrument.  In the ‘Spectrometer’ tab of the Instrument Status window, increase the PMT voltage until energy meter reading is between 70% and 80%.  The ‘Auto Gain’ button next to the voltage text box does this adjustment automatically. Since you have just installed the Cu lamp. 

  3. Go to the ‘Lamps’ tab of the instrument status window, if it may not be in its optimum position. The ‘<’ and ‘>’ buttons will rotate the lamp turret counter clockwise and clockwise, respectively. When you have finished optimizing the lamp and turret position, click the ‘Save’ button. This will store the lamp turret position in the AI 1200’s memory so that the position can be restored the next time you
    select this lamp.

Ø Ignition

  1. Move to the ‘Atomizer’ tab of the instrument status window. Click the ‘Ignite’ button to start the flame.

  2. Check the connections for the fuel and oxidant. Make sure that the flame atomizer is properly installed and that the interlock cable is plugged in. Also, ensure that the air-acetylene burner head is installed.

For Graphite Furnace AAS

Choose Operation Mode and Method

  1. The determination of cadmium (Cd) using graphite furnace AAS will be used as an example of how to operate the AI 1200 system.

  2. If the connections of the graphite furnace system to the main instrument are correct, the software will automatically recognize that a graphite furnace work head is installed and the Graphite Furnace operation mode will be displayed in the ‘Instrument Status’ window.

  3. For a Cd analysis, install a Cd HCL into lamp position #1, hook up the proper power cord, and enter “Cd” into the element textbox for the first tab. The wavelength and maximum current for the Cd lamp are displayed under the element symbol: 228.80 nm and 20 mA.  If you wish to change the data associated with an element, click the ‘Data’ menu option at the top of the ‘Main Window’, choose ‘Element Data’, pick an element from the list, change the reference data, and then click ‘Save’.

  4. A list of methods related to Cd determination using graphite furnace AAS appears when you click the ‘Open’ icon found in the toolbar at the top of the ‘Main Window’. Select the method Cd. and the ‘Method’ window for Cd using graphite furnace AAS will open.

  5. As previously stated, changing the data in the ‘Method’ window does not change the actual settings of the instrument. To take effect, the instrument settings must be changed in the ‘Instrument Status’ window, as outlined below.

Setting up the Spectrometer

  1. After a lamp is selected, the ‘Select’ button is replaced by several alignment buttons. Adjust the vertical and horizontal lamp alignment screws on the lamp holder assembly back and forth until the energy meter reading (top right of ‘Main Window’) is at its maxiumum. If the energy reading becomes too high or too low, click on the ‘Auto Gain’ button (in the ‘Spectrometer’ tab) to automatically adjust the PMT voltage to 70-80%.

  2. Click the ‘Peak WL’ button to optimize the wavelength. This function scans a ± 1 nm region and finds the wavelength that gives the maximum energy signal. 

  3. Click the‘Calibrate’button to calibrate the monochromator. The peak wavelength as read by the monochromator is in the Current Wavelength textbox and the true wavelength  stored in the software is in the True Wavelength textbox

  4. If background correction is required, choose ‘Deuterium Lamp’ as the Background Correction mode in the ‘Lamps’ tab (if no background correction is required, choose ‘None’). The D2 lamp.is then lit and the energies of the D2 and Cd HCLs should be balanced (within about 20% of each other).  If the energies are not balanced, click the ‘Balance’ button to automatically balance the energy of the HCL and D2 lamps.  If the energies are still not balanced, adjust the HCL current, D2 lamp current and/or slit width and click the ‘Balance’ button again.  Repeat until the lamp energies are balanced.

  5. After the D2 and Cd HCL lamp energies are balanced, click on ‘Auto Gain’ to adjust the PMT voltage

  6. Once the ‘Instrument Status’ window has been filled, click the ‘Copy Instrument Settings to Method’ icon at the top of the ‘Method’ window to update all the method parameters. Finally, click the ‘Save’ icon button to save the method

Optimizing the Light Path through Graphite Tube

  1. The position of the light path through the graphite tube is adjusted by changing the position of the graphite furnace head using the vertical and horizontal adjustment knobs. 

  2. Remove the glass furnace cover and place a small piece of paper over the hole at the end of the tube closest to the light source. The image of the HCL should pass cleanly through the graphite tube. If you can see the HCL on the paper when you move it to the outside of the tube, adjust the position of the graphite furnace head until the image passes untouched through the tube.

Furnace Program (Heating Curve profile

With a graphite furance atomizer, a very small amount of liquid sample (between 0.5 and 1ml) is placed inside the center of the graphite tube. A heating program to atomize the sample consists of at least three principal steps.

Drying Step

The graphite tube is quickly heated to a temperature just below the boiling point of the solvent. Then the temperature is slowly ramped past the boiling point. This step gently evaporates the solvent and leaves the dried sample inside the tube.

Ashing Step (also known as the Charring Step)  

This step removes any dry or semi-dry matrix that is left over from the drying step. Chemical modifications can also be used. For example, addition of oxygen or hydrogen to the purge gas, can change the chemical nature of the analyte and remove some of the matrix. Ashing temperatures depend on the element being analyzed in its matrix.

Atomization / Excitation Step

At this stage, the sample is a dry solid at the bottom of the graphite tube. The sample is atomized by rapidly increasing the temperature of the tube. The AI 1200 can heat at a rate up to 3800 K/s. The required atomization temperature depends on the element being analyzed.

Single Run

  1. After the above instrument settings and method development have been completed, the operator may find it useful to perform a single run to view the signal profile and/or gather data to further optimize a method.

  2. Click on the flashing red and yellow square next to the energy meter window in the top right of the ‘Main Window’. This expands the energy meter window to included displays for absorbance (Abs) and concentration (Conc).  There are also four buttons: ‘Corr 0’, ‘Calibr’, ‘Auto Gain’, and ‘D2 Balnc’. Click the ‘Corr 0’ button to reset the Abs reading to zero.

  3. Now click the ‘Single Run’ icon in the ‘Main Window’ toolbar to bring up the Single Run window.  Manually inject a sample into the furnace and click ‘OK’.

  4. When the run is finished, the operator is given the options of saving the chart file and printing the chart.

  5. Close the Single Run window.

Batch Run Sample Analysis

Clicking on the Batch Run icon in the toolbar of the ‘Main Window’ will open the Batch Run window

  Batch Run window

  1. The upper half of the window is divided into four boxes and the lower half contains the sample sequence table.  The box on the far left displays the status of the selected sample row (the row with the arrowhead).  It displays information about the method, the element, the sample number and location, and the current replicate being analyzed.  It also displays the Abs, Conc, and RSD for the sample.  To select a different row of the table, click on the gray box next to the row.

Sample Analysis

Concentration Calibration

The concentration of the analyte in an unknown sample can be calculated by comparing the signals generated by a series of standards against the signals generated by the unknown sample. This is done by preparing a series of standards that will “bracket” the concentration range where the unknown sample(s) are expected to fall. 

A plot of signal response versus concentration is constructed for the standards and the curve that best fits the relation is drawn on the plot. Concentrations of unknown samples may then be determined from this plot by drawing a horizontal line from the signal response (y) of the unknown sample to the calibration curve, then drawing a vertical line from this intersection down to the concentration (x) axis.
