Introduction

This document outlines the testing procedure P1, which must be performed on an monthly basis as indicated by M1 in the above title block

The main sources of dissolved oxygen  in water are the atmospheric oxygen and the oxygen produced from photo process by the algae.

The solubility of atmospheric oxygen in fresh waters ranges from 14.6 mg/L at 0 oC to about 7mg/L at 35oC under 1 atmosphere pressure.

Significance

Dissolved oxygen measurements are vital for maintaining aerobic conditions  in natural waters  that receive pollutional matter and in aerobic treatment processes intended to purify domestic and industrial waste waters.

Dissolved oxygen determinations are used for the maintenance of dissolved oxygen levels that will support the desired aquatic life in a healthy condition at all times.

Determinations of dissolved oxygen serve as the basis of the BOD test.

Sampling

Samples must overflow  two or three times the volume of  the bottles to ensure collection of representative samples. Most samples for dissolved oxygen are collected in the field where it is not convenient to perform the entire determination, it is customary to 'fix' the samples immediately after collection.

Methods of Determining Dissolved Oxygen

The winkler or iodometric methods and its modification are the standard volumetric procedures for determining dissolved oxygen at the present time, the test depends upon that oxygen oxidizes Mn++ to a higher state of valence under alkaline NaOH conditions and that manganese in higher valences is capable of oxidizing I - to I2  under acid conditions H2SO4 thus the amount of I2 released is equivalent to the dissolved oxygen originally present. The iodine is measured with standard sodium thiosulfate Na2S2O3.5H2O solution and interpreted in terms of dissolved oxygen.

MnO2 +  2 I -  + 2 H2SO4 ----> Mn2+ + I2 + 2 H2O

 

 

Winkler Method

The reaction of  winkler procedure is as follows:

Mn++   +  2OH -   ---->   Mn(OH)2            White precipitate

• If no oxygen is present ----> White precipitate of Mn(OH)2 forms when MnSO4 and the alkali iodide reagent (NaOH+ KI) are added to the sample.

• If  oxygen is present in the sample then some of Mn2+ is oxidized to Mn4+ and  precipitates as brown hydrated oxide .  The reaction is usually represented as follows:

Mn++   +  2OH - + ½ O2 ----> MnO2  +   H2O

Mn(OH)2   +  ½ O2  ---->  MnO2  +   H2O 

The oxidation of Mn2+ to MnO2 sometimes called fixation of oxygen, vigorous shaking of samples for at least 20 seconds is needed. After shaking, the samples for a time sufficient to allow all oxygen to react, the sulfuric acid is added and the sample is now ready for titration with 0.025 M thiosulfate. The liberated iodine which is equivalent to the dissolved oxygen is measured by titration with 0.025 M Na2S2O3.5H2O.Under the low pH condition, MnO2 oxidizes I - to produce I2.

MnO2 +  2 I -  + 4H+ ----> Mn2+ + I2 + 2 H2O

N.B. The sample should be stoppered and shaken for at least 10 seconds.

Interference

The unmodified winkler method is subjected  to interference with different substance and specially the oxidizing agents such as nitrite and Fe3+ that are capable of oxidizing I- to I2 and produce results that are too high  to obtain a permanent end point. The reactions involved may be represented as follow :

2NO2-+ 2 I- + 4H+ ----> I2 + N2O2 +  2 H2O

N2O2 +  H2O +  ½ O2 ----> 2NO2-  +  2H+

Nitrite interference may be easily overcome by the use of sodium azide NaN3, it is most convenient  to incorporate the azide in the alkali-KI reagent.When sulfuric acid is added NO2- is destroyed and the following reaction occurs :

NaN3  + H+ ----> HN3 + Na+

HN3  +  NO2 - +  H+ ----> N2 + N2O +   H2O

By this procedure nitrite interference is eliminated and the method of determination reactions retains the simplicity of the original winkler procedure.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Safety Precautions

  • The sample must be kept away from direct sunlight.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is correctly labeled.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is free from dust and organic matter.

Tools and Equipment

Apparatus

One 300 ml  BOD Bottle, Pipettes (2mL- 3mL- 5mL).

Reagents

Manganous Sulfate Solution-Dissolve 364g of manganous sulfate MnSO4.H2O in distilled water, and dilute to1 liter.

Alkali-Iodide, Azide Reagent-Dissolve 500g of sodium hydroxide NaOH  and 135g NaI in distilled water and dilute to 1 liter. Add 10g NaN3 dissolved in 40mL distilled water

Sodium ThioSulfate Standard Solution (0.025M)-Dissolve 6.025g of Na2S2O3.5H2O in distilled water. Standardize with Bi-Iodate solution.

Standard Potassium Bi-Iodate Solution (0.0021M)-Dissolve 812.4mg KH(IO3)2 in distilled water, and dilute to1 liter.  

Starch Solution- Use either an aqueous solution or a soluble starch powder mixtures.

Sulfuric Acid -Concentrated sulfuric acid H2SO4, one milliliter is equivalent to about 3mL Alkali-Iodide, Azide Reagent.

Standardization

  1. Dissolve approximately 2g KI free from iodate in flask with 100mL to 150mL distilled water, add 1mL (6N) H2SO4 or few drops of conc. H2SO4 and 20mL Standard Bi-Iodate solution.

  2. Dilute to 200mL and titrate librated iodine with thiosulfate titrant adding starch toward the end of titration when pale straw color is reached. If the solutions are of equal strengths, 20mL (0.025M) Na2S2O3 is required, if not adjust the Na2S2O3 solution to (0.025M).

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Work To be Carried Out

 

Procedure

  1. Add 1mL MnSO4 solution to the sample collected in 250-300 mL bottle, followed by 1 mL alkali-iodide, azide reagent if pipettes are dipped into sample rinse them before returning them to the reagent bottles. Hold pipette tip just above liquid surface when adding reagent.

  2. Stopper carefully to exclude air bubbles

  3. Mix by inverting bottle a few times. When precipitate has settled sufficiently to approximately half the bottle volume, to leave clear supernate above the manganese hydroxide floc.

  4. Add 1mL conc. H2SO4.

  5. Restopper and mix by inverting several times until dissolution is complete.

  6. Take 100mL of the sample and titrate.

  7. Titrate with 0.025M Na2S2O3 solution to a pale straw color. Add a few drops of starch solution and continue titration to first disappearance of blue color.

 

 

Calculation

For titration of 100 mL sample, 1mL  (0.025M)  Na2S2O3 =  2 mg DO/L

mg O2/L   =  V × M × 8 × 1000 / V1

Where:

M  =  Molarity of sodium thiosulfate,

V   =  Milliliter of  Na2S2O3 titrated,

 8   =  Equivalent Weight of O2  and

V1 =  mL of sample (100).
