Introduction

Conductivity is determined by measuring the conductance, which is the  ratio of current to voltage of a conductivity cell immersed in the solution of interest.

The model 35 will display conductivity directly in sub–multiples of mhos/cm when used with any cell having a constant of 1.0/cm.

Tools and Equipment

Conductivity Meter YSI Model 35 Conductance Meter

 

 

  • Connect the instrument to an appropriate power source.

  • Connect the conductivity cell leads to the terminals marked "CELL".

  • Immerse the conductivity cell into the solution to be measured. The electrodes must be submerged and the electrode chamber must be free of trapped air. Tap the cell to remove any bubbles and dip it two or three times to assure  proper wetting.

  • Rotate the range switch to the lowest range position which results in a reading on the display.

  •  The conductance value of the solution is displayed on the meter.

  • Conductivity may be determined by multiplying the conductance value displayed by the cell constant  K.

  • If a measurement record is desired, the YSI Model 35 may be connected to any recorder or other voltage measuring device with a 10 k Ω or higher input impedance.

Cell Constant

The cell constant K is a factor which is used to determine resistivity or conductivity of a solution.

Cell constant is determined by the physical configuration of the cell and its electrodes.

Conductivity is determined by multiplying the conductance measured in mhos or siemens by appropriate cell constant K.

Cells with constants of 1.0/cm cells will give good results with solutions between 20 and 20,000 micromhos. Values below or above this range are best measured with constants of 10.0/cm cells for any value above 2,000 micromhos.

Safety Precautions

 

Cell Precautions

  • The cell must be clean before making any measurement.

  • The cell should be suspended in the solution in such away the electrode chamber should be free of trapped air.

  • The cell should be at least 1/4 inch away from any other object, including the walls or bottom of the solution container.

  • Stirring may be  necessary for highest accuracy in instrument especially in low-conductivity solutions.

  • The presence of electrical fields and stray currents caused by stirrer motors, heaters, etc., can cause difficulties in obtaining good measurement results.

  • The cell always be handled carefully to decrease the possibility of breakage or loss of calibration accuracy.

  • The cell should never be transferred from one type of solution to another without having first been carefully rinsed with deionized water.

  • Never store a dirty or contaminated cell.

Cleaning and Storage of Cells

  1. "Bathroom cleaner" will clean the cell adequately when a stronger cleaning preparation use solution of  1:1 isopropyl alcohol and 10N HCl.
    Note:         Cells should not be cleaned in aqua regia or in any solution known to etch platinum or gold.

  2. Fill or dip the cell  with the cleaning solution for two or three minutes.

  3. Remove the cell from the solution and rinse it several change of distilled or deionized  water.

  4. It is best store  conductivity cell in distilled water for 24 hours before use.

Temperature Correction

  1. The conductivity of electrolytic solutions varies with temperature, concentration and composition.   

  2. The temperature coefficient may have a value as high as 3%/°C and each conductive ion has a different  temperature coefficient.

  3. The conductivity of a solution is the conductivity it exhibits at 25°C.

  4. The temperature coefficient can be determined either from measurments of representative samples or from published data. If sample composition changes  the coefficient should be redetermined.

  5. The temperature coefficient is known, the conductivity at 25°C can be determined from the following equation:

                      k T

k 25   =   ------------

                 1+ α rT

Where                  

             k 25  =  Conductivity at 25°C.

             k T  =  Conductivity at  measurement temperature T.     

             α   =  Temperature coefficient of conductivity ( change per degree ).

            rT =  Difference between measurement temperature and 25°C ( T-25°C ).

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Procedure

  1. Install the cell and thermometer in the stopper so that both will be well submerged when the stopper is in place on the container.

  2. Clean the containers, cell, thermometer and stopper in deionized water.

  3. Fill both containers with the solution of interest.

  4. Rinse the cell and thermometer in one sample, then place the stopper in the second sample , checking that the cell and thermometer are submerged and no air bubbles are trapped in the cell.

  5. Place the sealed samples in the controlled bath and set the bath control to obtain reading of 25oC on the sample thermometer stir the sample and measure the solution conductivity and temperature.

  6. Heat the solution to the upper limit of expected sample temperature and measure the conductivity and temperature.

  7. Cool the solution to the lower limit of expected sample temperature and measure the conductivity and temperature.

  8. Plot the three conductivity/temperature points on graph paper. The three points should be approximately linear. If they are not, the data may contain errors or else the solution may not have a linear temperature coefficient. If there are errors, repeat steps 2-7 on a fresh sample.

  9. If the solution is linear, compute the temperature coefficient α by the formula :

                                      kT - k25

                        α   =  -------------

                                     k25 × rT
