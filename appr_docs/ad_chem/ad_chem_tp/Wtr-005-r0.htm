Introduction

 

 

This document outlines the testing procedures P1, which must be performed three timed a week as indicated by 3W in the above title block and according to the Directives of the Central Chemical Laboratories.

Atomic Absorption Spectrometry is used for determining  concentration of metals in solution, this by aspirating a solution sample into a flame and atomized. Most atomic instruments also are equipped for operation in an emission mode.

 

 

Significance

 

Atomic Absorption Spectrometry resembles emission flame photometry in that  a sample is aspirated into a flame and atomized. The major difference is that in flame photometry the amount of light emitted is measured, whereas in atomic absorption spectrometry a light beam is directed through the flame, into a monochromator, and onto a detector that measures the amount of light absorbed by the atomized element in the flame. The amount of energy absorbed in the flame is proportional to the concentration of the element in the sample over a limited concentration range.

 

 

Summary of Method

 

This method describes how to prepare and calibrate an atomic absorption spectrophotometer for the quantitative determination of elements in solution by flame atomization, and preparation of a calibration curve to establish the optimum calibration range for the instrument and analytical method being used.

 

 

Purity of Reagents

 

All reagents shall conform to the specifications of the committee on Analytical Reagents of the American Chemical Society, where such specifications are available. Other grades may be used, provided that the reagent is of high purity to permit its use without lessening the accuracy of the determination.

 

 

Sampling

 

Collect the sample in accordance with the applicable ASTM method as follows : Method D 1066,  specification D1192 and practices D3370.

 

 

 

Interferences

 

Chemical Interferences

 

Chemical interferences results from the lack of absorption by atoms bound in molecular combination in the flame., this happen when flame is not hot enough to dissociate the molecules or when the dissociated atom is oxidized immediately to a compound that will not dissociate further at the flame temperature. Such interferences may be reduced or eliminated by adding specific elements or compounds to the sample solution or using the nitrous oxide-acetylene flame can be useful in minimizing certain types of chemical interference encountered in the air-acetylene flame.

 

 

Background Correction

 

Molecular absorption and light scattering caused by solid particles in the flame can cause erroneously high absorption values resulting in positive errors. When such phenomena occur, use background correction to obtain accurate values.( Deuterium arc lamp with a metal cathode and when both the line source hollow-cathode lamp and the deuterium lamp are placed in the same optical path and are times hard, the broad band background from the elemental signal is subtracted electronically, and the resultant signal will be background-compensated.

 

 

Sensitivity, Detection Limits and Concentration Ranges

 

Sensitivity- It is defined as the metal concentration that produces an absorption of 1% ( an absorbance of approximately 0.0044 ).

Detection Limits- It is defined as the concentration that produces absorption equivalent to twice the magnitude of the background fluctuation.

Sensitivity and detection limits vary with instrument, the element determined, the complexity of the matrix, and the technique selected.

Optimum Concentration Range- It is usually starts from the concentration of several times the sensitivity and extends to the concentration at which the calibration curve starts to flatten. To achieve best results, use concentrations of samples and standards within the optimum concentration range of the spectrometer. Safety Precautions

 

  • The sample must be taken as the applicable ASTM methods D 3370-76.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is correctly labeled.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is free from dust and dirt.

  • The sample must be kept away from exposure to direct sunlight.

 

Environmental Recommendation

 

  1. Cleaning solvents such as Benzene, Xylene, acids are dangerous, so you have to wear breathing mask.

  2. Keep this solvents closed. Do not get it in the eyes, on the skin, or on clothing. Do not breathe them, use only in a hood.

  3. Place vents in the laboratory to protect personnel from toxic vapors, protects the instruments from corrosive vapors, and prevents flame stability from being affected by room drafts.

Tools and Equipments

 

Apparatus

 

Atomic Absorption Spectrometer

It is consists of a light source emitting the line spectrum of an element ( hollow cathode lamp ), a device for vaporizing the sample ( flame ), a means of isolating an absorption line ( monochromator or filter and adjustable slit ) and a photoelectric detector with its associated electronic amplifying and measuring equipment.

Burner

The most common type of burner is a premix, which introduces the spray into a condensing chamber for removal of large droplets. The burner may be fitted with a conventional head containing a single slot; a three- slot Boling head which may be preferred for direct aspiration with an air-acetylene flame; or a special head for use with nitrous oxide and acetylene.

Readout

Most instruments are equipped with microprocessors capable of integrating absorption signals over time and linearizing the calibration curve at high concentrations.

 

Lamps

Use one hollow- cathode lamp for each element being measured. Multi- element hollow-cathode lamps generally provide lower sensitivity than single- element lamps.

Pressure-Reducing Valves

Maintain supplies of fuel and oxidant at pressures somewhat higher than the controlled operating pressure of the instrument by using suitable reducing valves. Use a separate reducing valve for each gas.

Vent

Place a vent about 15-30 cm above the burner to remove fumes and vapors from the flame. A variable-speed blower is desirable for modulating air flow and preventing flame disturbance.

Work to be carried out 

 

Instrument Operation

 

  1. Select the instrument operating parameters including wavelength of the analyte, element line emission source, support and fuel gases, and slit width for the element being determined by referring to the instrument manufacture's instructions.

  2. Turn on the appropriate support and fuel gases, and ignite the burner.

  3. Aspirate water until thermal equilibrium is reached.

  4. Adjust the readout system to obtain zero absorbance ( 100% T, where T = transmittance ) while aspirating blank, continue the aspiration until a stable signal is obtained.

  5. Aspirate a calibration solution, containing the analyte at a concentration that will yield an absorbance of 0.2 to 0.4 and at a concentration that is anticipated to be within the linear absorbance range  of the method.

  6. Adjust the burner, support and fuel gases, and nebulizer for maximum absorbance.

  7. The instrument now is ready to operate. When analyses are finished, extinguish flame by turning off first acetylene and then air.

Preparation of Standards

  1. Prepare standard solutions of known metal concentrations in water with a matrix similar to the sample.

  2. Use standards that bracket expected sample concentration and are within the method's working range. Very dilute standards should be prepared daily from stock solutions in concentrations greater than 500 mg/l. Stock standard solutions can be obtained from several commercial sources.

Preparation of Sample

 

Analysis of Oil

 

1- Char- Ashing Technique:

 

Weight 200gm of oil and charred on a full-heat-controlled hot plate.

Charbonization of the fat is conducted on as high a heat as possible ( 300°C ) but below ignition

temperature, this process takes about 24 hours.

Charring is complete when the sample stops smoking and no longer appears oily. After charring,

dishes are ignited in a lined muffle furnace at 500°C for 16 hours. Ash from the oil is extremely fine

and dishes should be cooled before removing them from the muffle furnace.

The ash is dissolved in 10ml of H2SO4,  make acid digestion for 30min. and then carefully washed

with distilled water into a 25ml volumetric flash and diluted to specific volume.

 

2- Direct Solvent Method

 

Weight 10gm of oil into a 50ml flask and dilute to specific volume with organic solvent ( Xylene ) and mix.

 

 

Analysis of Solids

 

Weight accurately approximately 0.2gm of  solid sample into a 150ml beaker, add 20ml of aqua regia

( HCl: HNO3 , 3:1 v/v ), cover with a watch glass and heat gently on a hot plate.

After dissolution is complete, transfer to a 100ml volumetric flask and dilute to volume with deionized water.

Filter the solution through a fine-porosity sintered glass filter into a volumetric flask and dilute to a  specific volume with  deionized water.

Dilute this solution if necessary to bring the analyte concentration into the linear range.

Determine the concentration of the element of interest using the standard conditions for each element and

the routine procedure.

 

 

Analysis of Solution

 

Transfer 100 ml of sample solution into a beaker or flask and add 5ml (1+1) high purity HCl and analyze.

 

For all samples, make certain that the concentration of acid and matrix modifiers are the same in both samples and standards.

 

 

 

 

 

Dilute the sample solution  if necessary to bring the analyte concentration into the linear range.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Standardization

 

Select at least three concentrations of each standard metal solution to bracket the expected metal concentration of a sample.

Aspirate blank and zero the instrument, then aspirate each standard in turn into flame and record absorbance.

Prepare a calibration curve by plotting on linear graph paper absorbance of standards versus their concentrations.

For instruments equipped with direct concentration readout, this step is unnecessary.

 

 

Analysis of Samples

 

Rinse nebulizer by aspirating water containing 105 ml conc. HNO3/L.

Atomize blank and zero instrument.

Atomize sample and determine its absorbance,  then read concentration directly from the instrument readout.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Calculation

 

  1. Calculate the concentration of each metal ion, in µ g/L for trace elements, and in mg/L for more common metals. Alternatively, read concentration directly from the instrument readout.

  2. If sample has been diluted, multiply by the appropriate dilution factor.
