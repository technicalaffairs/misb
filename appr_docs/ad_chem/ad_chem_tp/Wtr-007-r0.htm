Introduction

This document outlines the testing procedures P1, which must be performed three times a week as indicated by 3W in the above title block, and according to the Directives of the Central Chemical Laboratories.

Emission spectroscopy using inductively coupled plasma is a rapid, sensitive, and convenient method for the determination of metals in water and wastewater samples. Dissolved metals are determined in filtered and acidified samples. Total metals are determined after appropriate digestion. Care must be taken to ensure that potential interferences are dealt with, especially when dissolved solids exceed 1500 mg/l

Significance

An ICP source consists of a flowing stream of argon gas ionized by an applied radio frequency field typically oscillating at 27.1 MHZ. This field is inductively coupled to the ionized gas  by a water-cooled coil surrounded a quartz " torch " that supports and confines the plasma. A sample aerosol  is generated  in an appropriate nebulizer and spray chamber  and is carried into the plasma through an injector tube located within the torch. The sample aerosol is injected directly into the ICP, subjecting the constituent atoms to temperatures of about 6000 to 8000°C. Because this results in almost complete dissociation of molecules, significant reduction in chemical interference is achieved.

The high temperature of the plasma excites atomic emission efficiently. Ionization of a high percentage of atoms produces ionic emission spectra. The light emitted from the ICP is focused onto the entrance slit of a monochromator that effects dispersion. The slit  is used to isolate a portion of the emission spectrum for intenisty measurement using a photomultiplier tube. The monochromator  uses a single exit slit / photomultiplier and  may use a computer-controlled scanning mechanism to examine emission wavelengths sequentially. The sequential approach provides greater wavelength selection while the simultaneous approach can provide greater sample throughout.

Summary of Method

This method describes how to prepare and calibrate an emission spectroscopy for the quantitative determination of elements in solution by inductively coupled plasma ( ICP ), and preparation of a calibration curve to establish the optimum calibration range for the instrument and analytical method being used.

 

 

 

Interferences

 

Spectral Interferences

Light emission from spectral sources other than the element of interest may contribute to apparent net signal intenisty. Sources of spectral interference include direct spectral line overlaps, broadened wings of intense spectral lines, ion-atom recombination continuum emission, molecular band emission, and stray ( scattered ) light from the emission of elements at high concentrations. Avoid line overlaps by selecting alternate analytical wavelengths. Avoid or minimize other spectral interference by choice of background correction positions. A wavelength scan of the element line region is useful for detecting potential spectral interferences and for selecting positions for background correction.

 

Non Spectral Interferences

Physical Interferences- are caused by change in the physical properties of samples, such as viscosity and surface tension. This usually occurs when samples containing more than 10% ( by volume ) acid or more than 1500 mg dissolved solids/ liter are analyzed using calibration standards containing  ≤ 5% acid. If physical interference is present, compensate for it by sample dilution.

High dissolved solids content also contribute to instrumental drift by causing salt buildup at the tip of the nebulizer gas orifice. Using prehumidified argon for sample nebulization lessens this problem. Better control of the argon flow rate to the nebulizer using a mass flow controller improves instrument performance.

 

Chemical Interferences- are caused by molecular compound formation, ionization effects, and thermo chemical effects associated with sample vaporization and atomization in the plasma. Normally these effects are not pronounced and can be minimized by careful selection of operating conditions. Chemical interferences are highly dependent on sample matrix and element of interest.

Safety Precautions

  • The sample must be taken as the applicable ASTM methods D 3370-76.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is correctly labeled.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is free from dust and dirt.

  • The sample must be kept away from exposure to direct sunlight.

Environmental Recommendation

  1. Cleaning solvents such as Benzene, Xylene, acids are dangerous, so you have to wear breathing mask.

  2. Keep this solvents closed. Do not get it in the eyes, on the skin, or on clothing. Do not breathe them, use only in a hood.

  3. Place vents in the laboratory to protect personnel from toxic vapors, protects the instruments from corrosive vapors, and prevents flame stability from being affected by room drafts.

 

 

 

 

 

Tools and Equipment

 

Apparatus

 ICP Source 

 

It is consists of a radio frequency ( RF ) generator capable of generating at least 1.1 KW of power, torch, copper coil, local coil, impedance matching network, nebulizer, spray chamber, and drain. High quality flow regulators are required for both the nebulizer argon and the plasma support gas flow. A peristaltic pump is recommended to regulate sample flow to the nebulizer.

 

Spectrometer

 

It may be of the simultaneous ( polychromator ) or sequential ( monochromator ) type with air-path, inert gas purged, or vacuum optics.

Reagents

Use reagents that are of ultra-high-purity  grade or equivalent. Redistilled acids are acceptable. Dry all salts at 105°C for one hour and store in a desiccators before weighing. Use deionized water prepared by passing water throw at least two stages of deionization with mixed bed cation / anion exchange resins. Use deionized water for preparing all calibration standards, reagents, and for dilution.  

Calibration Standards

Prepare mixed calibration standards for the elements need to determination containing the concentrations as the manual of the instrument by combining appropriate volumes of the stock solutions in 100 ml volumetric flasks. Add 2 ml ( 1: 1 HNO3) and 10 ml ( 1:1 HCl ) and dilute to 100 ml with water. Verify calibration standards initially using quality control standard; monitor weekly for stability.

Calibration Blank

Dilute 2 ml ( 1:1 HNO3 ) and 10 ml ( 1:1 HCl )to 100 ml with water. Prepare a sufficient quantity to be used to flash the system between standards and samples.

Work to be carried out 

 

Sample Preparation

 

Oil Samples:

 

Char- Ashing Technique:

  1. Weight 200gm of oil and charred on a full-heat-controlled hot plate.

  2. Charbonization of the fat is conducted on as high a heat as possible ( 300°C ) but below ignition temperature,   this process takes about 24 hours.

  3. Charring is complete when the sample stops smoking and no longer appears oily. After charring, dishes are ignited in a lined muffle furnace at 500°C for 16 hours. Ash from the oil is extremely fine and dishes should be cooled before removing them from the muffle furnace.

  4. The ash is dissolved in 10ml of H2SO4  make acid digestion for 30min. and then carefully washed with distilled water into a 25ml volumetric flash and diluted to specific volume.

Direct Solvent Method:

 

Weight 10gm of oil into a 50ml flask and dilute to specific volume with organic solvent ( Xylene ) and mix.

 

 

Solid Samples:

  1. Weight accurately approximately 0.2gm of  solid sample into a 150ml beaker, add 20ml of aqua regia ( HCl: HNO3 , 3:1 v/v ), cover with a watch glass and heat gently on a hot plate.

  2. After dissolution is complete, transfer to a 100ml volumetric flask and dilute to volume with deionized water.

  3. Filter the solution through a fine-porosity sintered glass filter into a volumetric flask and dilute to a specific volume with deionized water. Dilute this solution ( if necessary to bring the analyte concentration into the linear range).

  4. Determine the concentration of the element of interest using the standard conditions for each element and the routine procedure.

Solution Samples:

 

 

Transfer 100 ml of sample solution into a beaker or flask and add 5ml (1+1) high purity HCl and analyze. For all samples, make certain that the concentration of acid and matrix modifiers are the same in both samples and standards. Dilute the sample solution  if necessary to bring the analyte concentration into the linear range.

 

Operating Conditions 

  1. Follow manufacturer's instructions. Establish instrumental detection limit, precision, optimum background correction positions, linear dynamic range, and interferences for each analytical line.

  2. The Cu/Mn intensity ratio may be incorporated into the calibration procedure, including specifications for sensitivity and for precision keep daily or weekly records of the Cu and Mn intensities and/or the intensities of critical element lines.

Instrument Calibration 

  1. Set up instrument as operating conditions. Warm up for 30 min. Check alignment of plasma torch and spectrometer entrance slit. Make Cu/Mn or similar intensity ratio adjustment.

  2. Calibrate instrument according to manufacture's recommended procedure using calibration standards and blank

  3. Aspirate each standard or blank for a minimum 15s after reaching the plasma before beginning signal integration.

  4. Rinse with calibration blank or similar solution for at least 60s between each standard to eliminate any carryover from the previous standard.

  5. Before analyzing samples, analyze instrument check standard. Concentration values obtained should not deviate from the actual values by more than ± 5%.

Analysis of Samples

  1. Begin each sample run with an analysis of the calibration blank, then analyze the method blank. This permits a check of the sample preparation reagents and procedures for contamination.

  2. Analyze samples, alternating them with analysis of calibration blank.

  3. Rinse for at least 60s with dilute acid between samples and blank. After introducing each sample or blank let system equilibrate before starting signal integration.

  4. Examine each analysis of the calibration blank to verify that no carryover memory effect has occurred. If carryover is observed, repeat rinsing until proper blank values are obtained. Make appropriate dilutions and acidifications of the sample to determine concentrations beyond the linear calibration range.

  5. Analyze instrument quality control sample within every run. Use this analysis to verify accuracy and stability of the calibration standards. If any result is not within ± 5% of the certified value, prepare a new calibration standard and recalibrate the instrument.

  6. Calculate the concentration of the element in the sample as in the method of analysis. For instrument calibrated to read directly in concentration, measure the calibration and samples and obtain the concentration of the analyte from the readout system.

  7. If the sample has been diluted, multiply by the appropriate dilution factor.
