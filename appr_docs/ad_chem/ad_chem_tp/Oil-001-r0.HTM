Introduction

This document outlines the testing procedure P1, which must be performed every one year Y1 as indicated in the above title block and according to the Directives of the Central Chemical Laboratories.

This method covers the determination of the Flash Point by Pensky - Martens Closed Tester of fuel oils, lubricating oils, viscous materials, this method is not applicable to drying oils, solvent-type liquid waxes.

Note 1 :This method was adopted as a joint ASTM-IP standard in 1967.

Note2 : This method may be employed for the detection of contamination of lubricating oil by minor amounts of volatile material.

Significance          

A low flash point is an indication of the presence of volatile combustible products in the oil. Prolonged exposure of the oil to very high temperature under fault conditions may produce sufficient quantities of low molecular weight hydrocarbons to cause a lowering of the flash-point of the oil.    "Ref: IEC 422"

Summary of method

The sample is heated at a slow, constant rate with continual stirring. A small flame is directed into the cup at regular intervals with simultaneous interruption of stirring.

 Definition

The flash point is the lowest temperature at which application of the test flame causes the vapour above the sample to ignite.

Safety Precautions

  • The sample must be taken as MPIS document no. TD-SS-003-r1

  • Make sure the sample bottle is correctly labeled   

  • Make sure the sample bottle is free from dust and dirt.

  • The sample must be kept away from exposure to direct sunlight.

  • As oil sample must be free from water it has to be taken under dry and clear environmental conditions.

Environmental Recommendations

  • The test must be done in a suction chamber.

  • Using solvents such as benzene and other for cleaning the valve parts is dangerous, it will expose lunge to very hazardous and carcinogenic chemicals, you have to wear your berating mask.

  • Do not throw the sump oil in the sewage because this oil will prevent the sewage treatment process collect it in tight containers and return it back to the vendor to recycle it. Always remember that "this oil is load for your wastes but it's valuable material for them".

  • Avoid oil spillage to the ground.

Work to be Carried Out 

 Preparation of apparatus

Support the tester on a level, steady table. Unless tests are made in a draft-free room or compartment, it is good practice, but not required, to, surround the tester on three sides with a shield, each section of which is about 18 in (46cm) wide and 24 in (61cm) high. 
Procedure

  1. Thoroughly clean and dry all parts of the cup and its accessories before starting the test, being sure to remove any solvent which had been used to clean the apparatus.

  2. Clean the cup with the sample.

  3. Fill the cup with the sample to be tested to the level indicated by the filling mark.

  4. Place the lid on the cup and set the latter in the stove.

  5. Be sure to have the locating or locking device properly engaged.

  6. Insert the thermometer.

  7. Light the test flame and adjust it to 4 mm in diameter.

  8. Supply the heat at such rate that the temperature as indicated by the thermometer increases 5 to 6 oC /min

  9. Turn the stirrer 90-120 rpm, stirring in a downward direction.

  10. If the sample is known to have a flash point of 220oF (104 oC) or below, apply the test flame when the temperature of the sample is not higher than 30oF (17 oC) below the flash point, and there after at temperature reading that is a multiple of3.3oF (2oC).

    - Apply the test flame by operating the mechanism on the cover which controls the shutter and test flame burner so that   the flame is lowered into the vapour space of the cup in 0.5 sec, left in its lowered position for 1 sec, and quickly raised to its high position.
    - Don't stir the sample while applying the test flame.

  11. If the sample is known to have a flash point above 220oF (104oC) apply the test flame in the manner just prescribed at each temperature that is a multiple of 5oF (3oC),  beginning at a temperature not higher than 30oF (17oC) below the flash point.

  12. Record as the flash point the temperature read on the thermometer at the time the test flame application causes a distinct flash in the interior of the cup. Don't confuse the true flash point with the bluish halo that sometimes surrounds the test  flame at applications preceding the one that causes the actual flash.
