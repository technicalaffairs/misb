Introduction

Volatile and fixed solids in sludge are determined by evaporating a weighted sample on a steam bath. Unlike total solid matter in wastewater which is expressed in mg/L solids in sludge are expressed in terms of percent.

Significance

Determination of total suspended solids in water by filtration of a well mixed sample. The residue is ignited at 550°C ± 50°C to a constant weight. Results are reported as milligrams of total nonfilterable residue per liter of sample. The determination is useful in control of wastewater treatment plant operation because it offers a rough approximation of the amount of organic matter present in the solid fraction of wastewater, activated sludge, and industrial wastes.

Definitions

Fixed solids is the term applied to the residue of total, suspended, or dissolved solids after heating to dryness for a specific time at a specified temperature. The weight loss on ignition is called volatile solids.

 Safety Precautions

  • Store sample in resistant-glass or plastic bottles.

  • Begin analysis as soon as possible because it's impractical to be preserved the sample.

  • If  necessary refrigerate sample at 4°C up to time of analysis to minimize microbiological decomposition of solids.

  • Bring samples to room temperature before analysis.

    •

  • The temperature at which the residue is dried has an important bearing on results.

  • Each sample require close attention to desiccation after drying.

  • Some evaporation residues readily absorb moisture; rapid weighing is essential.

  • The glass fiber disk will fuse at slightly above 550°C; therefore, it's important not to exceed this temperature when the crucible is ignited.

b) Muffle furnace- For operation at 550oC ± 50oC,

c) Desiccator- Provided with a desiccant containing a color indicator of moisture concentration,

d) Analytical balance- 200 g capacity, capable of weighing to 0.1 mg,

e) Drying oven- For operation at 103 -105oC and

f) Steam bath.

Work to be Carried out

  1. Place an adequately-sized evaporating dish in a drying oven at 103 -105oC for 15 or 20 min. after cleaning, then air-cool slightly and put the dish in a desiccator for complete cooling before weighing. Weight dish and record as W1

  2. Pour a portion of the well-mixed sample ( 25 to 50 ml ) into the dish, then weight sample and dish  together. Record as W2

  3. Place dish on a steam bath and evaporate to dryness then put the dish in drying oven for 1h at
    103°C,     cool in a desiccator, and weight. Record the weight as W3

  4. After weighing the dish and dry solids, compute the percent total solids ( Equation1 ).

  5. Place dish from step 4 in muffle furnace at 550°C until sample is burned completely. Length of time for complete burning depends on size of sample.

  6. Cool slightly, then put dish in desiccator for complete cooling before weighing. Weight the dish and record as W4 . The loss of weight ( W3-W4 ) is the volatile matter. The percent volatile matter then may be computed ( Equation 2 ).

Calculation 

 

                                                            Weight of dry solids (W3- W1) × 100

( 1 ) Percent Total Solids =   -------------------------------     

                                                               Weight of wet sludge (W2-W1)

   

                                                                Weight of volatile solids (W3-W4)  × 100          

           ( 2 ) Percent Volatile Matter =  ----------------------------------                                                               Weight of dry solids  (W3-W1)

                                                   

                                                                Weight of ash (W4-W1)  × 100

( 3 ) Percent Fixed Matter =     -------------------------------------          

                                                               Weight of dry solids (W3-W1)   

 

 

 

 

Precision

Repeat cycle of igniting, cooling, desicating, and weighing until a constant weight is obtained or until weight change is less than 4% or 0.5 mg, whichever is less. Duplicate determinations should agree within 5% of their average.
