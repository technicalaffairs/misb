Introduction

The chlorination of water supplies and polluted waters serves primarily to destroy or deactivate disease-producing microorganisms. A secondary benefit, particularly in treating drinking water, is the overall  improvement in water quality.

Chlorination may produce adverse effects. Taste and odor characteristics of phenols and other organic compounds present in a water supply may be intensified. Carcinogenic chloroform may be formed.

To fulfill the primary purpose of chlorination and to minimize any adverse effects, it is essential that proper testing procedures be used with a for knowledge of the limitations of the analytical determination.

Chlorine applied in water in its molecular or hypochlorite form initially undergoes hydrolysis to form free chlorine consisting of aqueous molecular chlorine, hypochlorous acid and hypochlorite ion. At the pH of most waters, hypochlorous acid and hypochlorite ion will predominate. Free chlorine reacts with NH3 to form combined chlorine.

Significance

The iodometric method "B" is suitable for measuring total chlorine concentration for natural and treated waters greater than 1 mg /L but this method suffer from interferences.

Summary

Chlorine will liberate free iodine from potassium iodide KI solutions at pH 8 or less. The liberated iodine is titrated with a standard solution of sodium thiosulfate Na2S2O3 with starch as the indicator. Titrate at pH 3 to 4 because the reaction stoichiometric at natural pH due to partial oxidation of thiosulfate to sulfate.

Interferences

  • Oxidized forms of Mn interferes.

  • Reducing agents such as organic sulfides also interfere.

  • The acid titration is preferred because some forms of combined chlorine don't react at pH 7. Use only acetic acid for the acid titration; sulfuric acid H2SO4 will increase interferences; never use hydrochloric acid HCl.

Minimum Detectable Concentration

The Minimum detectable concentration approximates 40 µg Cl as Cl2/L if 0.01 N Na2S2O3 is used with a 1000-mL sample. Concentrations below 1mg/L cannot be determined accurately by this method.

Safety Precautions

  • Store sample in glass bottles.

  • The reagent used must be of sufficiently high purity  to permit its use without lessening the accuracy of determination.

  • The sample must be taken as the applicable ASTM methods D 3370-76.

  • Acids such as acetic acid and others are dangerous, so you have to wear breathing mask.

  •  Keep these acids closed. Do not get it in the eyes, on the skin, or on clothing. Don't breathe them, use only in a hood.

Tools and Equipment

Reagents

Acetic Acid- Conc. (glacial).

Potassium Iodide- KI, crystals.

Standard Sodium Thiosulphate Titrant  0.025 N- Dissolve 6.205 gm of Na2S2O3.5 H2O in 100 ml freshly boild distilled water then complete to 1000 ml and standardize against potassium bi–iodate or potassium dichromate after at least 2 weeks storage. This initial storage is necessary to allow oxidation of any bisulfite ion present.To saving the solution add 5 ml chloroform CHCl3 or 0.4 gm of NaOH  to minimize bacterial decomposition.

Starch Indicator Solution-  To 5 gm add a little cold water and grind in a mortar to a thin paste pour into 1 L of boiling distilled water, stir, and let settle over night. Use clear supernate. Note  for preservation of starch add 1.25 gm of salycilic acid and 4 gm of ZnCL2.

Standard Iodine Solution 0.1 N- Dissolve 40 gm KI in 25 ml chlorine–demand free water, add 13 gm resublimed to a 1-L volumetric flask and dilute to mark.

Dilute Standard Iodine Titrant 0.028 N- Dissolve 25 gm KI in a little water in a 1-L volumetric flask add correct amount of 0.1 N iodine solution exactly standardized to yield a 0.0282 N solution and dilute to 1 L with chlorine demand free water.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Work to be Carried out

Procedure

Volume of Sample 

  1. Select a sample volume that will require no more than 20 ml 0.01N Na2S2O3 and no less than 0.2 mL for the starch iodide end point.

  2. For a chlorine range of 1 to 10 mg/L use a 500 mL sample, above 10 mg/L, use proportionately less sample.

Preparation for Titration

Place 5 mL acetic acid to reduce pH between 3.0 and 4.0 in a flask, add about 1 gm KI, pour sample in and mix with a stirring rod.

Titration 

  1. Titrate away from direct sunlight. Add 0.025 N or 0.01 N Na2S2O3 from a buret until the yellow color of the liberated iodine is discharged.

  2. Add 1 mL starch solution and titrate until blue is discharged.

Blank Titration 

  1. Correct result of sample titration by determing blank contributed by oxidizing or reducing reagent impurities.

  2. Take a volume of distilled water corresponding to the sample used for titration, add 5 mL acetic acid, 1gm KI and starch solution.

  1.  If a blue color develops, titrate with 0.01 N or 0.025 N Na2S2O3 to disappearance of blue color and record result ( B is negative ).

  2.  If no blue color occurs, titrate with 0.0282 N iodine solution until a blue color appears. Back–titration with 0.01N or 0.025 N Na2S2O3 and record the difference ( B is positive ).

Before calculating the chlorine concentration, substract the blank titration of ( 1 ) from the sample titration or if necessary add the net equivalent value of the blank titration of ( 2 ).

Calculation 

For determining total available residual chlorine in a water sample
