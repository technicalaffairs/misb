 Standard Test Method for Dissolved Oxygen in Water Method E

(Thiosulfate Titration–Starch Indicator)

Using ASTM D888-66  (P1-M1)

Introduction

This document outlines the testing procedures P1, which must be performed on a monthly basis ( more than once ) as indicated by M1 in the above title.

This method is applicable to steam condensate, boiler feed water and industrial water having a low level of dissolved oxygen concentration. 

This document initiated by Chem. Gihan Abd EL-Samee Elsaiad & reviewed by department director Chem. Maha Mohamed Saad Hamouda, revised by quality control  Chemist Saad Sadeek Hasanen at EDEPCo, and approved by Eng: Awad M. Fatthy Head Sector of  Talkha Power Station.

Significance

This test is carried out for dissolved oxygen measurements in closed cooling water cycles and industrial waters with low level of oxygen content.

Summary of Method

The sample is collected in a BOD bottle. The free iodine liberated in an amount equivalent to the oxygen in the sample is titrated with thiosulfate using starch as an indicator.

Interferences

Reducing and oxidizing materials in the sample will interfere in this method. The former leads to low results and the latter to high results. The use of the proper amount of alkaline iodide will minimize error from reducing interferences.

Nitrite interferences are eliminated by routine use of sodium azide. Ferric iron interferes unless 1 ml of potassium fluoride solution is used, in which case 100 to 200 mg/l can be tolerated. Ferrous iron interference is eliminated by the use of potassium permanganate solution.

Safety Precautions

  • Make sure the sample bottle is correctly labeled.

  • Make sure the sample bottle is free from dust and dirt.

  • The sample must be kept away from exposure to direct sunlight.

  • The bottles used for BOD should be equipped with glass stopper that are ground to a point to prevent trapping of air when the stopper is inserted.

Tools and Equipment

Apparatus

Sample Bottle: One 300 ml BOD bottle having a raised lip around the neck and a glass stopper ground to a conical lower tip.

Pipette: 2 ml transfer pipette, and serological pipettes of 1.00 and 5.00 ml capacities graduated in 0.01 and 0.10 ml divisions, respectively.

Beaker:  1-liter beaker.

Buret: 10-ml buret with 0.01-ml divisions.

 Reagents

Manganous Sulfate Solution, MnSO4.H2O (364g/liter)- Dissolve 364 g of manganous sulfate in water, filter and dilute to1 liter. No more than  a trace of iodine should be liberated when the solution is added to acidified (KI) solution.

Potassium Iodide, Alkaline Solution (150g/liter)- Dissolve 700 g of potassium hydroxide KOH  in sufficient water to make approximately 700 ml of solution in a 1 liter volumetric flask and cool to room temperature. Dissolve 150 g of iodate-free KI, in 200 ml of water and mix with KOH solution in the volumetric flask. Dilute to approximately 900 ml with water, allow to cool and then add 10 g of sodium azide NaN3 dissolved in 40 ml water, dilute to 1 liter, mix, and store in a dark, rubber-stoppered bottle.

Sodium ThioSulfate Standard Solution ( 0.1N ), Dissolve 24.82 g of Na2S2O3.5H2O in boiled and cooled water and diluted to 1L. Preserve by adding 5 ml of chloroform. For a dilute standard titrating solution (0.005 N ) transfer 25.00 ml of 0.1 N  Na2S2O3.5H2O to a 500-ml volumetric flask. Dilute to the mark with water and mix completely.Do not prepare more than 12 to 15 h before use.

Starch Solution

Sulfuric Acid (3:1) Conc- Pour carefully 750 ml of concentrated sulfuric acid (H2SO4,sp gr 1.84 ) into 250 ml of water in a beaker. Cool to room temperature, transfer to a 1 liter volumetric flask, and dilute to the mark with water.

Purity of Water

All reagent should conform to the specifications of the committee on Analytical reagents of the American chemical society where such specifications are available. Other grades may be used, provided that the reagent is of high purity to permit its use without lessening the accuracy of the determination.

Work to be Carried out

Sampling

  1. Collect the sample in a 300 ml BOD bottle in accordance with the sampling below:
    Place the sampling vessel in the sampling bucket and collect the sample under water using stainless steel or glass tubing and short pieces of neoprene tubing for connections where required. Do not use copper tubing or long sections of rubber tubing.

    Adjust the sample flow between 500 and 1000 ml /min maintaining sample temperature below 70oF (21oC) and below room temperature. Reduce the sample flow to 100 to 200 ml /min when using 60 ml sample tubes. Introduce the flowing sample into the bottom of a scrupulously clean sample vessel with a small tube. Spin the sample vessel several times to dislodge air bubbles or film adhering to the glass wall. Allow the sample to continue overflowing from the vessel for several more minutes.

  2. Wet the stopper and then slowly withdraw the glass tube while the sample continues to flow through it. As soon as the tip of the tube clears the liquid surface, ease the stopper into the neck and let it float down into its seat.

  3. Twist the stopper in tight, invert the bottle and inspect for air bubbles, if any can be seen, discard the sample and collect another.

Fixing

  1. Fill a 2 ml transfer pipette with alkaline potassium iodide solution, and with the pipette filled to the tip so that no bubble of air will be forced into the sample, ease the bottle stopper out of its seat and simultaneously thrust the pipette tip past it and into the neck of the bottle.

  2. Allow the contents of the pipette to drain into the bottle and as the level in the pipette approaches that in the bottle raise the pipette out of the bottle and let the stopper fall back into its seat.

  3. Fill another 2 ml pipette with MnSO4 solution and add this to the sample in the same way. Seat the stopper tightly, and shake or rotate the bottle to mix the contents thoroughly. Allow the bottle to stand, when the precipitate has settled below the shoulder of the bottle add 2 ml of H 2SO4 (3+1). In the same way seat the stopper and shake until all the precipitate is dissolved.

  4. Fixing should be completed within 15 min after sampling.

Titration of Sample

  1. Empty the cup around the stopper of the bottle and wash it with water. If the temperature of the sample is above 70oF (21oC), cool it by immersing the bottle in a bath of cracked ice.

  2. Drain the sample which shall be at temperature not above 70oF (21oC), into the clean beaker and add 10 drops of Starch indicator solution. Fill the burette in accordance with the volume required for the titration, with (0.005 N) Na2S2O3 solution. Start the motor stirrer if available; otherwise, stir constantly with a clean glass rod.

  3. Titrate to the disappearance of the blue, starch iodine color, rinsing the tip of the burette in the sample after each addition as the end point approaches. Complete titration within 30 min after sampling.

Calculation

1. Calculate the dissolved oxygen content of the sample in parts per million as follows:

    Dissolved Oxygen, ppm = 8000×N×S/V

    Where:

       N = normality of the Na2 S2O3 Solution (normality 0.005N),

       S =  milliliters of Na2 S2O3 Solution required for titration, and

       V = milliliters of sample used.

2. State the method used when reporting result.

Precision

Where this method is applicable, an experienced analyst can obtain results with a precision of 0.002 ppm of Oxygen.
