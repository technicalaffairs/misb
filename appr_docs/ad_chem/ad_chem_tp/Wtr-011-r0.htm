Standard Test Methods for Chemical Oxygen Demand ( Dichromate Oxygen Demand) of Water

Test Method B- Micro COD by Sealed Digestion and Spectrometry

Introduction

This document outlines the testing procedures P1, which must be performed on a monthly basis as indicated by M1 in the above title block, and according to the Directives of the Central Chemical Laboratories.

This test method  is essentially equivalent to Test Method A, but it utilize micro volumes of the same reagents contained in a sealable ampule or a screw-top culture tube and a spectrophotometer to measure absorbance or transmittance at selected wavelengths. This test method is applicable  where only small sample volumes  are available and where large numbers of samples need to be analyzed.

This test method is requires less space per analysis and uses less of the reagents, minimizing costs and volume of wastes discharged.

Summary of Method

The dichromate reagent and silver catalyst used in this test method are similar to those used in Test Method A, but the volumes employed are1/20th of those in Test Method A.

A sample aliquot is introduced carefully into an ampule or a screw-top culture tube so that the sample is layered on the top of previously  introduced reagents and remains there until the ampule or tube is sealed. This technique limits evolution of heat of solution until the container is sealed, minimizing the loss of volatile organics.

After sealing, the ampule or tube is heated in an oven, sand bath,or heated block at 150 ± 2°C for 2 h. The COD concentration is determined spectrophotometrically after digestion. In the low COD range(5 to approximately 50 mg/L), the loss of hexavalent chromium is measured at 420 nm, while for the high range (50 to approximately 800 mg/L), the increase in trivalent chromium is measured at 600 nm. The ampule or tube serves as the absorption cell.

Significance and Use

This test method is used to chemically determine the maximum quantity of oxygen that could be consumed by biological or natural chemical processes due to impurities in water. typically this measurement is used to monitor and control oxygen-consuming pollutants, both inorganic and organic, in domestic and industrial wastewaters.

Definition

Oxygen Demand-The amount of oxygen required under specified test conditions for the oxidation of waterborne organic and inorganic matter.

Interferences

Interferences identified in the test method A are also applicable to the micro procedure. Volatile materials will be lost if the sample is mixed with the reagents before the ampule or tube is sealed. Volatile materials will also be lost during sample homogenization.

Potentially, the loss of volatile organics in the micro procedure will be less than  that which occur in Test Method A. Thus, results between the two methods may differ if volatile materials are involved.

Spectrophotometric interferences may exist due to turbidity of precipitated salts that are too colloidal to settle in a reasonable period of time.

Sampling

Collect the sample in accordance with Practices D 3370-76.

Preserve samples by cooling to 4°C if analyzed within 24 h after sampling, or preserve for up to 28 days at 4°C and at pH < 2 by addition of concentrated sulfuric acid. The addition of 2 ml of concentrated sulfuric acid per litre at the time of collection will generally achieve this requirement. The actual holding time possible without significant change in the COD may be less than 28 days, especially when easily oxidizable substances are present.

Safety Precautions

  • Exercise extreme care when handling concentrated sulfuric acid, especially at the start of the refluxing step.

  • Silver sulfate is poisonous; avoid contact with the chemical and its solution.

  • Mercuric sulfate is very toxic; avoid contact with the chemical and its solution.

  • Cleaning solvents such as acid dichromate, nitric acid and others are dangerous, so you have to wear breathing mask.

  • Keep this solvents closed. Do not get it in the eyes, on the skin, or on clothing. Do not breathe them, use only in a hood.

  • If handling the ampule or tube directly with insulated gloves, or place the ampules or tubes in a rack for min. use normal laboratory precautions for possible contact with the hot corrosive reagents from broken ampules or tubes.

Tools and Equipment

Apparatus

 

Spectrophotometer

Suitable for measurements at 600 nm and 420 nm using the ampules or culture tubes as absorption cells.

Heating Oven

Sand bath, or block heater capable of maintaining a temperature of  150 ± 2°C throughout. If an oven is used and screw-top tubes are employed, ascertain that the caps can withstand the oven temperature and solution pressure. The heating device must be equipped with a high temperature shutt-off set at 175 to 185°C.

 

Cultures Tubes

Borosilicate glass,16by100 mm, with TFE-florocarbon-lined screw caps and culture tubes from dust contamination.

Ampules

Borosilicate glass,10 ml, may be substituted for the culture tubes. These ampules are rotated and uniformly sealed with a glass blowing torch after addition of sample and reagent solutions. The nominal path length of these ampules shall be 15 to 20 mm.

Apparatus for Blending or Homogenizing Samples

A tissue homogenizer is recommended.

Reagents

 

Silver Sulfate  Catalyst Solution

Dissolve 22 g of silver sulfate (Ag2SO4) in a 4.09 Kg (9 Ib) bottle of concentrated sulfuric acid.

Ferrous Ammonium Sulfate Solution (0.1N)

Dilute 400 ml of  0.25 N ferrous ammonium sulfate solution,{ Dissolve 98.0 g of  ferrous ammonium sulfate solution( FeSO4. (NH4)SO4.6H2O) in water. Add 20 mL of sulfuric acid ( sp gr 1.84), cool and dilute to1 L}. Standardize  against 0.25 N potassium dichromate K2Cr2O7. To standardize, dilute 25 ml of 0.25 N of potassium dichromate solution ( K2Cr2O7) to about 250 ml. Add 20 ml of  sulfuric acid ( sp gr 1.84) and allow the solution to cool. Titrate with the  ferrous ammonium sulfate solution to be standardized, using the phenanthroline ferrous sulfate indicator.

 

Calculate the normality as follows:

 

 

                                                                  N = ( A × B ) / C

Where:

N  =  normality of  the  ferrous ammonium sulfate solution,

 

 

A  =  potassium dichromate  solution, ml,

 

 

B  =  normality of  the potassium dichromate  solution, and

 

 

C  =  ferrous ammonium sulfate solution,  ml.

Ferrous Ammonium Sulfate Solution (0.01N)

Dilute 40 ml of 0.25 N ferrous ammonium sulfate solution to 1 L. Standardize against 0.025 N of potassium dichromate solution.

Phenanthroline Ferrous  Sulfate  Indicator Solution

Dissolve 1.48 g of 1,10-(ortho)-phenanthroline monohydrate, together with 0.70 g of ferrous sulfate (FeSO4.7H2O), in 100 ml of water. This indicator may be purchased already prepared. If desired, the indicator may be diluted 1:5 for use in this test method.

Potassium Dichromate Digestion Solution 

High Range- Add 10.216 g of potassium dichromate, (K2Cr2O7) dried at 103°C for 2 h, 167 ml of  concentrated sulfuric acid ( sp gr 1.84) and 33.3 g of Mercuric Sulfate ( HgSO4 ) to about 750 ml of water, mix, and let cool. Dilute the solution to 1 L with water and mix thoroughly.

 

Low Range- Add 1.022 g of potassium dichromate, (K2Cr2O7) dried at 103°C for 2 h,167 ml of  concentrated sulfuric acid ( sp gr 1.84) and 33.3 g of Mercuric Sulfate ( HgSO4 ) to about 750 ml of water, mix, and let cool. Dilute the solution to 1 L with water and mix thoroughly.

 

Potassium Acid Phthalate  Solution Standard (1ml = 1mg COD)

Dissolve 0.851 g potassium acid phthalate ( KC8H5O4 ), primary standard, and dilute to 1 L in water.

Use the next procedure for  the determination of absorbance of  each standard and blank for high COD range, and low COD.

 

Prepare calibration curves for each range by plot the absorbance of each standard on the abscissa and gm/L of COD on the ordinate. For the low procedure, the correlation will have a negative slope; while for high range procedure, the slope is positive.

Work to be carried out 

  1. Place 1.5 ml of digestion solution for  the high range procedure or for low range procedure in a culture tube or glass ampule.

    Note 1- Accurate addition of the digestion volume in the low range procedure is important because the loss of hexavalent chromium is measured.
     

  2. Add 3.5 ml of silver sulfate catalyst solution, mix, and allow to cool. If the mixed reagents are to be store the sealed or capped solution in the dark.

  3. Homogenize the sample if necessary.

  4. Carefully add 2.5 ml of the sample, standar or blank down the side of the tube or ampule so that a layer is formed on top of the reagent. Cap the tubes or seal ampules.

  5. Mix the sealed  ampules or tube thoroughly. It is feasible to mix tubes by holding the tube by the cap and shaking vigorously. Complete integrity of the TFE flow-carbon liner in the screw cap is imperative. The ampule tube will become hot because of heat of solution.

  6. After mixing, place the ampules or tubes in an oven or heating device at 150 ± 2°C for 2 h.

  7. Allow the ampules or tubes to cool at room temperature. After about 5 min, mix the contents of the ampule or tube thoroughly ( to mix condensed water into the solution). Thereafter, permit the solution to cool and permit precipitated solids to settle (normally about 30 min). Rapid cooling will generate colloidal precipitates that are difficult to settle.

  8. Make spectrophotometric readings using the absorption cells.

  9. Measure the absorbance of the low range solutions at 420 nm and the high range solutions at 600 nm.

Calculation

Calculate the COD value directly from the respective calibration curves.

If the sample was prediluted, apply the appropriate dilution factor to the result.

Report all results in mg/L.
